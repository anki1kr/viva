01 Conflict Resolution & Group Discussion UNIT IV

Conflict is unavoidable in teams. The mark of a good communicator is not avoiding conflict but managing it through structured strategies. Group Discussion is the recruiter's favourite tool to test these skills under time pressure.

1.1 What is Conflict?

Definition Conflict is a disagreement, struggle or clash that arises when two or more individuals or groups perceive their interests, goals, values or needs as incompatible. It can be task-based (what to do), process-based (how to do it) or relational (personal friction).

Elements of conflict

ElementMeaning
PartiesThe individuals or groups involved.
IssueThe actual disagreement or incompatible goal.
PerceptionHow each side sees the issue (often more important than the issue itself).
EmotionAnger, fear, frustration, pride that intensifies the dispute.
PowerWho controls resources, authority or information.
OutcomeThe resolution — win-win, win-lose, lose-lose or stalemate.
Healthy / Functional Conflict
Sparks new ideas, surfaces hidden problems, builds stronger team bonds when resolved well.
Unhealthy / Dysfunctional Conflict
Personal attacks, low morale, missed deadlines, broken trust, attrition.
Types of Conflict
Conflict scales from inside one person (intra-personal) all the way to between organisations (inter-group).

1.2 The 3 P's of Conflict Resolution

Definition Every conflict can be analysed through three lenses — Problem, People, Process. Address all three, in that order, and the conflict resolves cleanly.
PFocusKey QuestionAction
ProblemThe actual issueWhat exactly is wrong?Define the issue in neutral, factual terms — separate it from personalities.
PeopleThe humans involvedWho is affected and how do they feel?Acknowledge emotions, listen actively, respect viewpoints.
ProcessThe path to resolutionHow will we decide?Agree on rules, timeline, decision method (vote, consensus, escalation).
Example — 3 P's in action Two teammates argue about a project deadline.
  • Problem: Module X missed its date by 3 days.
  • People: Dev feels overworked; PM feels ignored. Both heard.
  • Process: Daily 10-min stand-up + shared Kanban for next sprint.
Memory hook "Fix the Problem, Honour the People, Follow a Process." — Skip any one and the conflict comes back.

1.3 The 5 Conflict Resolution Strategies (Thomas–Kilmann)

Each strategy is a different mix of assertiveness (pursuing your goal) and cooperativeness (helping the other side reach theirs).

Thomas–Kilmann Conflict Mode Grid
Each strategy = a different blend of assertiveness × cooperativeness. Compromise sits in the middle.
StrategyStyleUse WhenRisk
AvoidLow / LowIssue is trivial, emotions are too high right now, more info needed.Problem festers if used long-term.
AccommodateLow / HighOther side cares more, you were wrong, preserving the relationship matters more than winning.You lose voice; resentment builds.
CompeteHigh / LowEmergency, unpopular but correct call (safety, ethics, legal).Damages relationships, breeds rebellion.
CompromiseMid / MidEqual-power parties, deadline pressure, temporary fix.Nobody fully satisfied; weak solution.
CollaborateHigh / HighStakes high for both sides; long-term relationship; novel solution possible.Time-consuming; needs trust.
Example — All five in one scenario Two team leads want the same conference room at 3 PM.
  • Avoid: "Let's deal with it tomorrow." (Skip — meeting is today.)
  • Accommodate: "You take it, I'll book another." (When their meeting has external clients.)
  • Compete: "I booked first, here's the ticket." (When the booking system is authoritative.)
  • Compromise: "Use it 3–3:30, I'll take 3:30–4." (Both have shorter meetings.)
  • Collaborate: "Let's video-conference together — same topic anyway." (Best outcome.)

Advantages of structured conflict resolution

Better decisions — multiple viewpoints surface options.
Stronger relationships — addressed disputes build trust.
Higher productivity — less time lost to silent feuds.
Personal growth — emotional intelligence improves.
Innovation — friction generates fresh ideas.
Reduced attrition — staff stay where conflict is handled.
Memory hook "A CAT is a Cat" — Avoid, Compete, Accommodate, Compromise, CollAborate. Five strategies, two extremes plus a middle.

1.4 Group Discussion (GD) — Basics

Definition A Group Discussion is a structured conversation among 6–12 candidates on a given topic, used by recruiters and admissions panels to assess communication, knowledge, leadership, teamwork, and reasoning under pressure — within a fixed time (usually 10–20 minutes).

Elements of a Group Discussion

Anatomy of a GD
A GD has six interlocking parts — remove any and the discussion collapses.

Topic categories you must prepare

Regional issues National policy International affairs Abstract topics Case studies Current affairs

What examiners look for

SkillBehaviour that earns marks
ContentFacts, examples, statistics, balanced viewpoints.
CommunicationClear voice, correct grammar, appropriate volume, body language.
LeadershipInitiating without dominating, inviting silent members, summarising.
Team-buildingAcknowledging others' points, building on ideas ("Adding to what Riya said…").
Critical thinkingQuestioning assumptions, offering alternative angles, evidence-based.
ListeningEye contact, nodding, referencing earlier points correctly.

Advantages of Group Discussion

Tests many skills at once — communication, knowledge, leadership.
Saves time — recruiters screen 10 candidates in 20 minutes.
Reveals real behaviour under pressure, not rehearsed answers.
Encourages teamwork — selfish dominators stand out negatively.
Generates ideas — pooled brain-power produces better solutions.
Builds confidence — practice in public speaking.
Pitfalls — what kills your GD score Interrupting · Personal attacks · Dominating airtime · Sitting silent for the whole GD · Repeating points already made · Bluffing facts you don't know · Speaking too fast · Aggressive body language.
Tip — Open or summarise The two highest-impact moments are the first 30 seconds (set the frame) and the last 30 seconds (summarise the discussion). Doing either well almost guarantees selection.
Example — Strong GD opener Topic: "Should AI be regulated by the government?"
You: "Good morning. Before we take a side, let's frame the question — 'AI' covers everything from spam filters to weapons. I propose we focus on three fronts: privacy, employment, and safety. Privacy first?"
Why it works: defines scope, structures the discussion, invites others.

Quick Check — Topic 1

Q1. List the 3 P's of conflict resolution.
Problem (the actual issue), People (those involved & their emotions), Process (the agreed path to resolution).
Q2. Which strategy is high-assertiveness AND high-cooperativeness?
Collaborate — both parties pursue full satisfaction through a creative win-win.
Q3. When is "Avoid" the right strategy?
When the issue is trivial, when emotions are too hot to discuss productively right now, or when more information is needed before responding.
Q4. Name two GD behaviours that earn negative marks.
Interrupting/dominating other speakers, and personal attacks (or bluffing facts, sitting silent, repeating points).
Q5. State two advantages of Group Discussion as a selection tool.
Tests multiple skills at once (communication, leadership, teamwork) and saves time by screening many candidates in parallel under realistic pressure.

02 Interview Skills — STAR & Mock Interviews UNIT III

Interviews are structured conversations where employers test fit, skills, and behaviour. The STAR framework gives crisp answers to behavioural questions; mock interviews rehearse the whole experience until it feels natural.

2.1 What is an Interview?

Definition An interview is a formal, two-way conversation in which an interviewer assesses a candidate's knowledge, skills, personality, and culture-fit for a role, while the candidate evaluates the role and organisation. It is purposeful, structured, and time-bound.

Types of interviews

TypeWhat it testsExample question
StructuredSame questions to every candidate — fairness."Walk me through your resume."
UnstructuredFree-flowing — personality & thinking."Tell me about yourself."
BehaviouralPast behaviour predicts future behaviour."Tell me about a time you failed."
TechnicalJob-specific knowledge & problem-solving."Reverse a linked list in O(n)."
Stress / CaseComposure under pressure."Estimate the number of barbers in Delhi."
Panel / GroupMultiple interviewers or candidates at once."Discuss this case among yourselves."

Elements of a successful interview

Preparation — research the company, role, common questions.
Appearance — appropriate dress, grooming, punctuality.
Body language — eye contact, posture, firm handshake.
Communication — clear, concise, structured answers.
Listening — fully understand each question before answering.
Follow-up — thank-you note within 24 hours.

2.2 STAR Method — The Four Steps

Definition STAR stands for Situation, Task, Action, Result — a structured way to answer behavioural questions by describing what was happening, what you had to do, what you actually did, and the measurable outcome.
STAR Method — Answer Flow
Action gets the lion's share — that's where your skills, decisions, and judgement actually show.
StepStands ForPurposeWhat to include
SSituationSet the scene — when, where, who.Brief context (1–2 sentences). Project name, team size, timeline.
TTaskDefine your specific responsibility.The challenge or goal you owned. Avoid "we" — use "I".
AActionDescribe the steps you took.Bullet 2–4 concrete actions. This is the longest part — 60% of the answer.
RResultShow the outcome with numbers.Quantified result + lesson learned. "Reduced bug count by 40%."
Memory hook "Set The Action Rolling" — Set the scene, Tell the task, Act, Result.

2.3 Role of STAR in Interviews

Why interviewers prefer STAR answers
  • Provides structure — no rambling; answer fits in 90 seconds.
  • Reveals real behaviour — past actions predict future actions better than hypothetical answers.
  • Quantifies impact — the Result step forces metrics, not vague claims.
  • Comparable across candidates — same framework = fair scoring.
  • Builds candidate confidence — pre-rehearsed STAR stories reduce blank-mind moments.

2.4 Worked Example — STAR in Action

Example — "Tell me about a time you handled a tight deadline"

Situation: "In my final-semester project, our 4-person team was building an e-commerce site. Three days before submission, our payment gateway integration broke."

Task: "As the backend lead, I was responsible for restoring payments before the demo."

Action: "I (1) isolated the failing endpoint using Postman tests, (2) found the API key had expired, (3) registered a sandbox key, (4) added a retry-with-backoff wrapper, and (5) wrote two unit tests so it wouldn't break silently again."

Result: "Payments were live in 6 hours, the demo passed without issue, and the project scored 92/100 — the highest in our batch."

Common STAR mistakes Saying "we did X" instead of "I did X" · skipping the Task step · giving Result without numbers · letting Situation eat 70% of the answer · choosing a story where you weren't actually the actor · negative results without lessons.

2.5 Mock Interviews — Benefits, Roles, Significance

Definition A mock interview is a simulated interview conducted by a friend, mentor, professor, or career coach that mimics a real interview — same questions, dress code, time pressure — followed by detailed feedback. It is the single most effective way to convert preparation into performance.
Mock Interview — Roles & Cycle
Three roles produce one feedback loop — and the candidate runs the cycle again until smooth.

Roles in a mock interview

RoleResponsibility
CandidateTreats it as the real thing — formal dress, no notes, full effort.
InterviewerStays in character, asks varied questions, follows up like a recruiter would.
Observer / RecorderNotes body language, filler words, eye contact, time per answer; records video if possible.
Mentor / CoachSynthesises feedback into 2 strengths + 2 gaps + 1 action item.

Benefits of mock interviews

BenefitHow mock interviews build it
CommunicationForces you to verbalise thoughts in real time, fix filler words ("umm", "like").
ConfidenceRepeated exposure desensitises nerves; you stop fearing the unknown.
Problem-solvingMock panels throw curveballs — you practise thinking aloud under pressure.
Body languageRecordings reveal habits (slouching, eye-contact gaps) you can correct.
Time managementYou learn 90-second answers feel right, 4-minute answers feel rambling.
Feedback loopEach mock gives a critique; real interviews don't.
STAR fluencyStories you've told three times come out clean on interview day.

Significance — why mock interviews matter

Reduces interview anxiety — familiarity breeds composure.
Identifies weak spots safely, before stakes are real.
Improves articulation of complex projects in plain language.
Refines self-presentation — voice, posture, dress.
Builds a story bank of 6–8 STAR stories you can deploy.
Boosts placement-rate — proven by every campus career cell.
Example — Mock interview workflow Day 1: 30-min mock with senior + recording. Day 2: review video, note 3 issues (filler words, weak result step, no eye contact). Day 3: rerun the same questions targeting the 3 fixes. Day 5: fresh mock with new questions + new observer. Repeat weekly until placement season.

Quick Check — Topic 2

Q1. What does STAR stand for?
Situation, Task, Action, Result.
Q2. Which step should occupy ~60% of a STAR answer and why?
Action — because it shows the interviewer your actual skills, decisions, and behaviour. The other steps are context or outcome.
Q3. State the role of STAR in interviews.
It gives candidates a clear structure for behavioural answers, surfaces past behaviour (the best predictor of future behaviour), forces measurable outcomes, and lets recruiters score candidates fairly using the same template.
Q4. Name three roles in a mock interview.
Candidate, Interviewer, Observer/Recorder (and optionally a Mentor/Coach for synthesis).
Q5. Give two benefits of mock interviews.
Builds confidence through repetition; provides a feedback loop on body language and filler words; sharpens STAR delivery; reduces anxiety on interview day. (Any two.)

03 Content Analysis & Articulation UNIT II

Unit II turns you into a critical reader: comprehend a source, judge its credibility (CRAAP), detect bias, and evaluate arguments with evidence. These are the skills behind every research paper, news report, and policy brief.

3.1 Meaning of Content Analysis & Articulation

Definition — Content Analysis Content Analysis is the systematic study of a piece of communication (article, speech, video, social-media post) to identify its main idea, evidence, structure, intent, and underlying assumptions. The goal is to understand what is being said, how, and why — and to judge whether it is reliable.
Definition — Articulation Articulation is the ability to express ideas clearly, precisely and persuasively in speech or writing — so the audience grasps the meaning without effort. Strong articulation = right words + logical order + appropriate tone for the audience.

Why these two go together

You first analyse a source (input) and then articulate your response (output). Analysis without articulation stays trapped in your head; articulation without analysis is empty noise.

Content Analysis → Articulation Pipeline
Five stages turn raw input into a defensible, well-expressed view.

Three-step comprehension recipe

Skim headings → Scan for evidence (numbers, quotes, citations) → Summarise the thesis in one sentence.

3.2 Identifying Credible Sources

Definition — Credible Source A credible source is one whose information is accurate, current, supported by evidence, and produced by a qualified author with no hidden motive. Citing credible sources is the foundation of academic and professional writing.

Quick credibility checklist (before deep CRAAP)

  • Author's name and credentials are visible.
  • Publisher is a known institution (university, government, peer-reviewed journal, established news outlet).
  • Claims are backed by citations or data.
  • Date of publication is recent enough for the topic.
  • Tone is balanced, not emotional or sensational.
  • URL ends in .edu, .gov, .org (caution: .org can still be biased) — not just a personal blog.

3.3 The CRAAP Test — Source Credibility

Definition The CRAAP test is a five-criterion checklist developed by Sarah Blakeslee at CSU Chico for evaluating whether a source of information is credible enough to cite or act on.
CRAAP Test — Five Criteria
All five criteria must pass for a source to be safely cited.
LetterCriterionQuestion to askRed flag
CCurrencyWhen was it published / last updated?Outdated stats, dead links.
RRelevanceDoes it directly address my topic and audience?Off-topic, wrong reading level.
AAuthorityWho is the author / publisher? Credentials?Anonymous post, no affiliation.
AAccuracyIs the evidence verifiable? Citations? Peer-reviewed?No sources, factual errors.
PPurposeWhy was it written — to inform, sell, persuade, entertain?Hidden sponsorship, propaganda.
Memory hook "Don't swallow CRAAP without checking the 5 C's" — Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose.
Example — CRAAP applied A blog claiming "AI will take 90% of jobs by 2025":
  • C: Posted 2018 → outdated. ✗
  • R: Topic matches if writing about AI labour impact. ✓
  • A: Author = "Tech Guru Joe", no credentials. ✗
  • A: No links, no studies cited. ✗
  • P: Page covered with affiliate links to a course → selling. ✗
Verdict: Reject — 4 of 5 fail.

Advantages of using CRAAP

Saves time — five quick checks weed out junk fast.
Avoids embarrassment — citing fake stats kills your grade.
Builds research discipline — habit for life.
Beats fake news — works on social media too.

3.4 Bias and Eliminating Bias

Definition Bias is a systematic tendency to favour or disfavour a viewpoint, person, or group in a way that distorts judgment. Bias can live in the writer, the source, or the reader.

Common types of bias

TypeDescriptionMini-example
Confirmation biasAccepting evidence that supports your view, ignoring evidence that contradicts.Reading only news sites that match your politics.
Selection biasCherry-picking sources or data points.Quoting only the 3 surveys that agree with you, ignoring 7 that don't.
Cultural / political biasFraming news through one ideology.Same event called "protest" by one paper, "riot" by another.
Sponsorship / funding biasFindings that conveniently match the funder's interest.Sugar-industry-funded study finds sugar harmless.
Survivorship biasStudying only winners, ignoring failures."All these CEOs dropped out of college, so dropping out works."
Anchoring biasFirst number/idea unduly influences later judgment."Original ₹999, now ₹399!" feels cheap even if ₹399 is overpriced.

Strategies to identify and eliminate bias

  1. Cross-reference with at least two independent sources from different perspectives.
  2. Check the funding and affiliations of the publisher.
  3. Look for emotional language ("disastrous", "miraculous") — facts use neutral words.
  4. Separate fact from opinion — underline verifiable claims.
  5. Seek the counter-argument deliberately (steel-manning).
  6. Use peer-reviewed or primary-source data wherever possible.
  7. Check your own bias — would you accept this evidence if it argued the other side?
Tip — How bias hurts arguments A biased premise produces an unsound conclusion even when the logic is valid. Garbage in → garbage out. Examiners reward students who flag and remove bias from their sources.

3.5 Evaluating Arguments

Definition An argument is a set of one or more premises offered as reasons to support a conclusion. Evaluating an argument means checking whether the premises are true and whether they actually support the conclusion.

Elements of an argument

Anatomy of an Argument
A sound argument needs true premises AND a valid logical link to the conclusion.

Validity vs Soundness

Valid

The conclusion follows logically from the premises — even if the premises are actually false.

Example: "All birds can swim. A penguin is a bird. ∴ A penguin can swim." → Valid form, false premise.

Sound

The argument is valid AND its premises are true. Sound arguments give certain (deductive) or strong (inductive) conclusions.

Example: "All humans are mortal. Socrates is human. ∴ Socrates is mortal."

Steps to evaluate an argument

  1. Identify the conclusion — what is the writer trying to convince me of?
  2. List the premises — what reasons are being offered?
  3. Check the premises — are they true? CRAAP-credible?
  4. Check the inference — do the premises actually lead to the conclusion?
  5. Look for fallacies — see common ones below.
  6. Consider counter-arguments — is there evidence the writer ignored?
  7. Decide — accept, reject, or accept with reservations.

Common logical fallacies (auto-fail signs)

FallacyWhat it doesExample
Ad hominemAttacks the person, not the argument."You can't trust her economic plan — she failed maths in school."
Straw manMisrepresents the opponent's view to attack a weaker version."You want school reform? So you want to ban teachers!"
Slippery slopeClaims one step inevitably leads to extreme outcomes."If we allow flexible hours, soon nobody will work at all."
Appeal to authorityCites a famous figure outside their expertise."This actor says the vaccine is unsafe."
False dilemmaPresents only two options when more exist."Either we cut welfare or the economy collapses."
Hasty generalisationBig claim from tiny sample."My two friends from Pune are loud → all Pune people are loud."
Circular reasoningConclusion is hidden in the premise."The book is true because it says so itself."
Example — Evaluating an argument Claim: "We should switch to a 4-day work week because Iceland tried it and productivity rose."
  • Conclusion: Adopt 4-day week.
  • Premise: Iceland's pilot improved productivity.
  • Premise check (CRAAP): Verified — multi-year study, peer-reviewed. ✓
  • Inference check: Does one country's success in public-sector trials imply universal success? Hasty generalisation risk.
  • Counter-argument: Different industries, work cultures, salary structures.
  • Verdict: Accept with reservations — needs sector-specific pilots before scaling.

Why assess alternative viewpoints?

Reduces confirmation bias — see weaknesses in your own view.
Strengthens reasoning — anticipate counter-arguments.
Builds empathy — useful in negotiation, GD, leadership.
Improves decisions — multiple perspectives surface options.
Demonstrates honesty — examiners reward this.
Avoids fallacies — false dilemmas die quickly.

Quick Check — Topic 3

Q1. Define content analysis in one sentence.
The systematic study of a piece of communication to identify its main idea, evidence, structure, intent and underlying assumptions, and to judge its reliability.
Q2. Expand CRAAP.
Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose.
Q3. State two ways to eliminate bias.
Cross-reference with independent sources representing different perspectives; check funding and affiliations; separate fact from opinion; deliberately seek the counter-argument; use peer-reviewed sources. (Any two.)
Q4. Difference between a valid and a sound argument?
A valid argument has a logical structure where the conclusion follows from the premises (even if premises are false). A sound argument is valid AND has true premises.
Q5. Name two common logical fallacies.

04 Six Thinking Hats — Edward de Bono UNIT I (SESSIONAL)

Edward de Bono's Six Thinking Hats is a parallel-thinking technique that lets a team explore a decision from six distinct angles — one at a time — eliminating ego clashes and producing richer, more balanced ideas.

4.1 What is the Six Thinking Hats Method?

Definition A parallel-thinking technique invented by Maltese psychologist Edward de Bono (1985) where the team mentally "wears" one coloured hat at a time, so everyone analyses the issue from the same angle simultaneously. The hats represent different modes of thinking — facts, feelings, caution, optimism, creativity, and process.
Why "parallel" thinking works Traditional meetings devolve into adversarial thinking — one person criticises while another defends, wasting energy. Parallel thinking lines everyone up to explore the same dimension at the same time, then move on together. The result: every angle gets coverage, no one's ego is bruised.

4.2 The Six Hats — Elements

de Bono's Six Thinking Hats
Six modes of thinking — wear one hat at a time, all together.
HatModeFocusSample question
⚪ WhiteFactsData, information, what we know vs what we need."What numbers do we actually have?"
🔴 RedFeelingsGut reactions, emotions, intuition — no justification needed."How do I feel about this idea right now?"
⚫ BlackCautionRisks, weaknesses, why it might fail (devil's advocate)."What could go wrong?"
🟡 YellowOptimismBenefits, value, why it will work."What's the best-case outcome?"
🟢 GreenCreativityNew ideas, alternatives, lateral thinking."What if we did the opposite?"
🔵 BlueProcessManages the thinking — agenda, summaries, next hat."Which hat should we wear next?"

4.3 How to Run a Six-Hat Session

  1. Blue hat first — leader sets the question, agenda, and time per hat (3–5 min each).
  2. White hat — gather all known facts and identify gaps.
  3. Red hat — quick gut reactions (no debate).
  4. Yellow hat — list every benefit and supporter.
  5. Black hat — list every risk and weakness.
  6. Green hat — generate alternatives, mash-ups, wild ideas.
  7. Blue hat (close) — summarise, decide, list next steps.
Memory hook "Wear White, Read Black; Yellow Grows Better" — White (facts), Red (feel), Black (risk), Yellow (benefit), Green (create), Blue (boss).

4.4 Worked Example — Should our college start a coding club?

Example — Six Hats applied
  • ⚪ White: 60% of placed students last year were from CS branches; we have 4 free hours weekly.
  • 🔴 Red: Faculty feel excited; some students fear it'll burn time before exams.
  • ⚫ Black: Risk of becoming clique-y; instructors may not be available; burnout near exams.
  • 🟡 Yellow: Boosts placements, builds portfolio, fosters peer learning, free for college.
  • 🟢 Green: Run as monthly hack-nights instead of weekly; rotate student leads; pair with industry mentors via LinkedIn.
  • 🔵 Blue: Pilot for one semester with 20 students, monthly reviews, decision in April.
Outcome: balanced, defensible decision in 30 minutes — versus a free-for-all argument that goes nowhere.

4.5 Advantages of the Six Thinking Hats

Eliminates ego clashes — everyone wears the same hat at once.
Saves time — focused 5-min rounds beat 1-hour rambles.
Surfaces every angle — no blind spots.
Lets shy members speak — Red hat invites pure feeling.
Encourages creativity — Green hat is dedicated time for wild ideas.
Improves decisions — risks (Black) and benefits (Yellow) get equal airtime.
Easy to learn — colours map to roles in minutes.
Works in any field — used by IBM, NASA, schools, hospitals.

4.6 Limitations / Pitfalls

When the hats fail Without a strong Blue-hat facilitator, sessions drift · forcing a hat when no input fits feels artificial · cultural settings where direct disagreement is taboo (Black hat) need careful framing · over-using the technique for trivial decisions wastes time.

Quick Check — Topic 4

Q1. Who invented the Six Thinking Hats and in what year?
Edward de Bono, 1985.
Q2. What does the Black hat focus on?
Caution — risks, weaknesses, reasons an idea might fail (devil's advocate role).
Q3. Which hat manages the process and decides which hat comes next?
Blue hat — the facilitator hat that handles agenda, summaries and next steps.
Q4. State two advantages of the Six Thinking Hats method.
Eliminates ego clashes by aligning everyone to one mode at a time; surfaces every angle (facts, feelings, risks, benefits, creativity, process) so decisions aren't lopsided. Other valid: saves time, lets shy members contribute, easy to learn.
Q5. Why is the Red hat important?
It legitimises raw feelings and intuition with no need to justify them — capturing emotional data that would otherwise stay hidden and later sabotage the decision.

6.2 Section B (split) — 4-Mark Mini Answers

Twelve mini-answers for the half-of-8-mark slots. Format: definition (1 line) → 3-4 bullet structure → tiny example. Roughly 4–6 lines each.

Q1 · 3 P's of Conflict (4M)

Definition. Every conflict has three lenses — Problem, People, Process — and clean resolution requires fixing all three in order.

  • Problem: name the issue in neutral facts.
  • People: acknowledge emotions, listen actively.
  • Process: agree decision rules & timeline.

Example: Missed deadline → fix the slipped module (P), hear dev's overload (P), set daily stand-up (P).

Q2 · Functional vs Dysfunctional Conflict (4M)

Definition. Functional conflict improves outcomes; dysfunctional conflict damages them.

  • Functional: task-focused, surfaces ideas, builds trust.
  • Dysfunctional: personal, emotional, breaks team morale.
  • Convert: separate person from problem; use 3 P's.

Example: Two devs debating frameworks (functional) vs the same two refusing to talk (dysfunctional).

Q3 · Compromise vs Collaborate (4M)

Definition. Both are middle-to-high cooperative styles, but only one is true win-win.

  • Compromise: both give up something — quick, partial.
  • Collaborate: both gain — needs time and trust.
  • Use compromise under deadline; collaborate when stakes & relationship are both high.

Example: Splitting a meeting room 30/30 (compromise) vs merging the meetings (collaborate).

Q4 · GD Evaluation Criteria (4M)

Definition. Recruiters score five observable skills during a Group Discussion.

  • Content: facts, data, structure of arguments.
  • Communication: clarity, fluency, tone.
  • Leadership: initiation, summarising, including others.
  • Teamwork & body language round out the score.

Tip: Open or summarise — first & last 30 seconds carry the most marks.

Q5 · STAR Method (4M)

Definition. A four-step structure for behavioural interview answers.

  • Situation — context (1-2 lines).
  • Task — your responsibility.
  • Action — what you did (60% of answer).
  • Result — quantified outcome.

Example: "Server crashed in fest registration (S); I led recovery (T); switched to backup DB & pushed hotfix (A); 800 users registered, zero data loss (R)."

Q6 · Mock Interview Benefits (4M)

Definition. A simulated interview followed by structured feedback to rehearse the real one.

  • Builds confidence & reduces anxiety.
  • Polishes communication & body language.
  • Surfaces weak STAR answers before the real interview.
  • Provides safe, low-stakes feedback loop.

Roles: candidate, interviewer, observer, mentor.

Q7 · CRAAP Test (4M)

Definition. A 5-criterion source-credibility checklist by Sarah Blakeslee.

  • Currency — is it recent?
  • Relevance — does it fit my topic?
  • Authority — qualified author / publisher?
  • Accuracy — evidence-backed, verifiable?
  • Purpose — inform or persuade / sell?

Example: Wikipedia article fails Authority but may pass Currency & Relevance.

Q8 · Types of Bias & Removal (4M)

Definition. Bias is a systematic distortion that unfairly favours one side.

  • Confirmation: seeking only agreeable info.
  • Selection: cherry-picking sources/data.
  • Cultural / Authority: background or status colours judgement.
  • Remove by: cross-referencing, peer review, fact vs opinion separation.

Example: Reading only one news outlet on an election → confirmation bias.

Q9 · Valid vs Sound Argument (4M)

Definition. An argument's logical strength is measured on two dimensions.

  • Valid: conclusion follows correctly from the premises.
  • Sound: valid and all premises are actually true.
  • Every sound argument is valid; not every valid one is sound.

Example: "All cats fly; Tom is a cat; Tom flies" — valid but not sound (premise false).

Q10 · Top 5 Logical Fallacies (4M)

Definition. Errors in reasoning that look persuasive but are logically flawed.

  • Ad hominem — attack person, not argument.
  • Straw man — distort opponent's view.
  • Slippery slope — small step → catastrophe.
  • False dilemma — only two options offered.
  • Hasty generalisation — sweeping claim from few cases.
Q11 · Six Hats — Roles (4M)

Definition. Six coloured "hats" represent six modes of thinking used one-at-a-time (parallel thinking).

  • White = facts, Red = feelings, Black = caution.
  • Yellow = benefits, Green = creativity, Blue = process.
  • Whole group wears the same hat at the same time.

Mnemonic: "WhiRe Yellow Bla-Gree Blu" → W R Y B G B.

Q12 · Content Analysis & Articulation (4M)

Definition. Two paired skills: analyse a source critically, then articulate the conclusion clearly.

  • Content analysis: identify thesis, evidence, intent, reliability.
  • Articulation: structure, clarity, audience-fit, persuasion.
  • Together they convert raw information into communicated insight.

Example: Reading two news reports on a budget → summarise, compare, present in class.

4-Mark template (memorise) "DEF · 3-bullet structure · 1 mini-example" — fits in any 4-mark slot.

6.3 Section B — 8-Mark Full Answers

Seven model answers, each sized for a full 8-mark slot. Format: definition → labelled table or diagram → worked example → advantages.

Q1 · Explain the 3 P's of Conflict Resolution with a worked scenario. (8M)

Definition The 3 P's of Conflict Resolution — Problem, People, Process — are three lenses every conflict must be examined through. Skip any one and the conflict resurfaces.
3 P's of Conflict — Triangle Model
Each vertex feeds into a balanced resolution at the centre. Drop any vertex and the triangle (and the deal) collapses.
PFocusKey QuestionAction
ProblemThe actual issueWhat exactly is wrong?State it neutrally; separate from personalities.
PeopleHumans & emotionsWho is affected and how?Acknowledge feelings, listen, respect viewpoints.
ProcessPath to resolutionHow will we decide?Agree on rules, timeline, decision method.
Worked scenario — Hostel mess committee dispute Two students argue about menu changes.
  • Problem: Vegetarian students feel ignored on Sunday menu.
  • People: Veg group feels unheard; non-veg group feels accused. Both are heard before decisions.
  • Process: Mess committee + warden vote weekly; rotating menu chair + feedback box.
Outcome: balanced rotation accepted, no further escalations.

Advantages

Holistic — covers issue, emotion & method.
Reusable — works at home, classroom, workplace.
Calms tempers — "People" lens releases emotion early.
Prevents recurrence — Process locks the fix in.

Q2 · Describe the 5 Conflict Resolution Strategies (Thomas–Kilmann) with one common scenario applied to all five. (8M)

Definition The Thomas–Kilmann model places every conflict response on two axes — assertiveness (pursuing your goal) and cooperativeness (helping the other side) — yielding five distinct strategies.
StrategyStyleBest UseRisk
AvoidLow / LowTrivial issue or boiling emotions.Problem festers.
AccommodateLow / HighOther side cares more; preserve relationship.You lose voice.
CompeteHigh / LowEmergency, ethical or safety call.Damages trust.
CompromiseMid / MidEqual power, time pressure.Weak shared solution.
CollaborateHigh / HighHigh stakes, long relationship, time available.Time-consuming.
Worked scenario — Two team-leads claim the same conference room at 3 PM
  • Avoid: "Postpone to tomorrow." (Bad — meeting is today.)
  • Accommodate: "Take it; I'll book another." (Good when their meeting has external clients.)
  • Compete: "I booked first; here's the receipt." (Use when authority is clear.)
  • Compromise: "You take 3:00–3:30, I take 3:30–4:00." (Both shrink scope.)
  • Collaborate: "Both meetings are about Q3 launch — let's merge." (Best — true win-win.)
Memory hook "A CAT is a Cat" — Avoid, Compete, Accommodate, Compromise, CollAborate.

Advantages

Diagnostic — name the style being used.
Flexible — different strategy per situation.
Builds EQ — forces self-awareness.
Reduces stalemate — provides a vocabulary.

Q3 · Explain the STAR method with a fully worked answer to "Tell me about a time you failed". (8M)

Definition STAR — Situation, Task, Action, Result — is a behavioural-interview structure that converts past experience into a measurable, examiner-friendly story. About 60% of the answer should sit in Action.
STAR Answer Template — Box Layout
Action is the largest box — 60% of words. Situation & Result are short and sharp.
Worked answer — "Tell me about a time you failed"
  • Situation: "In my second year I led a 4-member team in the inter-college hackathon (24-hour challenge)."
  • Task: "We had to build a working e-waste reporting prototype and present it."
  • Action: "I scoped too aggressively — full mobile app + admin dashboard + ML categoriser. Hour 18 we realised none of it was demo-ready. I called a stop, dropped the ML piece, redivided work, and we finished only the reporting flow with a static admin view."
  • Result: "We scored 4th of 22 teams instead of placing top-3. I logged a personal post-mortem; in the next hackathon I pre-cut scope at hour 4 and we won 2nd place."
Notice: failure stated honestly, ownership taken, lesson quantified.

Why STAR scores high

Structure — examiner can map every sentence.
Evidence — past behaviour, not promises.
Measurable — Result is quantified.
Honest — even failures look professional.

Q4 · Explain the CRAAP test and apply it to a sample web source. (8M)

Definition CRAAP (Sarah Blakeslee, 2004) is a 5-criterion checklist to judge a source's credibility before citing it: Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose.
CriterionQuestionPass signal
CurrencyWhen was it published / updated?Recent enough for the field.
RelevanceDoes it directly fit my topic & level?Audience and depth match.
AuthorityWho wrote/published it? Credentials?Named author, qualified, reputable publisher.
AccuracyAre claims evidence-backed and verifiable?Citations, data, peer review.
PurposeWhy does it exist — inform, persuade, sell?Transparent, neutral intent.
Worked example — applying CRAAP to "blog.unknown.io/AI-jobs-2026.html"
  • Currency: Posted 2024 — marginal for fast-moving AI job data.
  • Relevance: Topic matches "AI & employment" — pass.
  • Authority: Anonymous author, no "About" page — fail.
  • Accuracy: Numbers given but no citations or links — fail.
  • Purpose: Page hosts ads for an AI bootcamp — likely persuade/sell.
Verdict: 1 pass / 4 fail → reject; find a peer-reviewed or government-statistics source instead.

Advantages

Quick — five questions, two minutes.
Universal — books, sites, social media, papers.
Defensive — protects you from misinformation.
Citable — examiners recognise it as standard.

Q5 · Explain Edward de Bono's Six Thinking Hats with a worked group decision. (8M)

Definition The Six Thinking Hats (Edward de Bono, 1985) is a parallel-thinking framework where the whole group "wears" the same coloured hat at the same time — each colour standing for one mode of thinking. This avoids ego-clashes and gets every angle on the table.
HatModeAsks…
WhiteFacts & dataWhat information do we have / need?
RedFeelings & intuitionWhat is my gut reaction?
BlackCaution & riskWhat could go wrong?
YellowOptimism & benefitsWhat are the upsides?
GreenCreativity & ideasWhat new options exist?
BlueProcess & controlWhat is our agenda & next step?
Worked example — "Should our college start a coding club?"
  • Blue: Agenda — 10 min, decide go/no-go this week.
  • White: 240 CSE students; 18 colleges in city already have one; ₹0 budget approved.
  • Yellow: Skill-building, placements, peer network, brand for college.
  • Black: Faculty bandwidth, attendance dropouts, no funding for events.
  • Green: Online-only kick-off, partner with senior alumni, weekend hackathons.
  • Red: Most students "excited"; faculty "cautious".
  • Blue (close): Decision — start as informal study group, formalise after one semester.
Memory hook "WhiRe Yellow Bla-Gree Blu" — White, Red, Yellow, Black, Green, Blue. Or remember FFCBCP = Facts, Feelings, Caution, Benefits, Creativity, Process.

Advantages

Removes ego — no "my idea vs yours".
Saves time — parallel, not adversarial.
Full coverage — emotion & logic both heard.
Works in 6–60 minutes — scales with depth.

Q6 · What is a Group Discussion? Explain its evaluation criteria with a diagram. (8M)

Definition A Group Discussion (GD) is a structured, time-bound conversation among 6–12 candidates on a given topic, used by recruiters and admissions panels to assess communication, knowledge, leadership, teamwork and reasoning under pressure in a single 10–20 minute observation.
GD Evaluation Rubric — Weighted Bar Chart
Content + Communication together account for nearly half the score — prepare facts and clarity first.
CriterionWhat examiners look for
ContentRelevant facts, recent data, structured arguments.
CommunicationClarity, fluency, tone, audience-fit.
LeadershipInitiation, summarising, including silent members.
TeamworkListening, respecting turns, building on others.
Body languagePosture, eye-contact, controlled gestures.
Top scoring tactic Open or summarise — the first 30 seconds and the last 30 seconds carry disproportionate marks. If you cannot open, aim to summarise.

Advantages of the GD format

Multi-skill — tests 5 traits at once.
Time-efficient — 10 candidates in 20 minutes.
Realistic — mirrors workplace meetings.
Comparative — direct peer-to-peer ranking.

Q7 · Explain Mock Interviews — purpose, roles, cycle and significance. (8M)

Definition A mock interview is a simulated practice interview followed by structured feedback. It rehearses real interview pressure in a low-stakes setting so that real-time mistakes are surfaced and fixed before the actual interview.
Mock Interview — Practice Cycle
Four-phase loop: prepare → simulate → feedback → refine — repeat until performance plateaus.

Roles in a mock interview

RoleResponsibility
CandidateTreats it as real; uses STAR; accepts feedback.
InterviewerAsks mix of HR + technical + behavioural questions.
ObserverNotes filler words, posture, eye contact, time per answer.
Mentor / FacultyDiagnoses patterns, recommends next-loop focus.
Worked snapshot Round-1 mock: candidate over-uses "basically", jumps to Action without Situation. Observer flags both. Round-2 mock: candidate prefaces every answer with one-line context and drops "basically". Confidence visibly higher in round-3.

Significance & benefits

Confidence — pressure becomes familiar.
Communication polish — filler words drop.
Body-language fix — posture & eye-contact improve.
STAR fluency — answers become structured.
Anxiety reduction — fewer surprises on D-day.
Safe failure — mistakes cost zero offers.

1. Expand STAR and CRAAP.
STAR = Situation, Task, Action, Result. CRAAP = Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose.
2. Which conflict strategy is win-win and why?
Collaborate — high assertiveness + high cooperativeness; both sides' goals achieved through joint problem-solving.
3. Name the 6 hats and the mode each represents.
White=facts, Red=feelings, Yellow=benefits, Black=caution, Green=creativity, Blue=process control.
4. What are the 3 P's of conflict resolution?
Problem (the issue), People (humans & emotions), Process (path to resolution).

Content, Communication, Leadership (also: Teamwork, Body language).
