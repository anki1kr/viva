Unit 01 · de Bono's parallel thinking

U1Six Thinking Hats

One man-tool. Six lenses. Forces 360° thinking instead of stuck-in-one-mode arguing.

High-yield · 5 / 8 M

1.1 Six Thinking Hats — the full method 8 M

Scoring template: Def + author (1) · 6 hats (colour + role + key Q) — 4 · diagram — 1.5 · team-setting benefit + example — 1.5 · = 8 M
Concept Method invented by Edward de Bono for parallel thinking. Each "hat" represents one mode of thought. Whole group wears the same hat at the same time → everyone faces the same direction → no clash, full coverage.
tip — leader (Blue hat) controls the order: usually White → Yellow → Black → Red → Green → Blue close.

The six hats — colour, mode, key question

⚪ White · FactsPure data, info, stats. No emotion. Q: "What do we know? What's missing?"
🔴 Red · EmotionFeelings, hunches, gut. No need to justify. Q: "What's my reaction?"
⚫ Black · CautionRisks, flaws, pitfalls — the "devil's advocate". Q: "What can go wrong?"
🟡 Yellow · OptimismBenefits, value, opportunity. Q: "What's the upside? Why will this work?"
🟢 Green · CreativityNew ideas, alternatives, "what if". Q: "What else? Any other angle?"
🔵 Blue · ProcessManages flow, sets agenda, summarises. Q: "What's next? Where are we?"

How prof asks (paper pattern)

Q-style "Discuss Edward de Bono's Six Thinking Hats in detail. How can this method improve problem-solving and decision-making in a team setting?" → must list 6 hats and 4-5 team-benefit points with example.

Why it matters — 7 critical-thinking benefits

  • Well-rounded view — each hat surfaces a perspective you'd otherwise miss · cuts tunnel vision
  • Objectivity + emotional awareness — White forces facts only · Red lets emotion exit cleanly
  • Boosts creativity — Green hat asks "what else" · pushes past status quo
  • Risk + benefit balance — Black + Yellow together stop one-sided decisions
  • Group collaboration — everyone wears same hat at same time → no ego war
  • Process management — Blue hat keeps meeting on track · stops digressions
  • Mitigates bias — White forces data > assumption · Black exposes hidden negatives
Mnemonic: "WiReBlYeGrBu" — White-fact · Red-feel · Black-bad · Yellow-yes · Green-grow · Blue-boss.

Worked example — "Should we launch product X?"

Apply 6 hats White → sales projection · cost data.
Red → "team feels rushed".
Black → cash-burn risk if launch fails.
Yellow → first-mover advantage in segment.
Green → soft-launch beta to 10% of users first?
Blue → "let's vote, then schedule next sync".
examiner gold — open with "by Edward de Bono" + acronym + diagram = first 3 marks locked.

2.1 Content Analysis & Articulation 4 M

Content Analysis Research method — systematically code & categorise texts, images, videos or media → identify patterns, themes, meanings.
Articulation Process of clearly & effectively expressing ideas using language, tone & style → engage and persuade audience.

Compare — the input/output pair

tbl 2.1 · CA vs articulation
AspectContent AnalysisArticulation
DirectionInput — decode others' contentOutput — encode own ideas
GoalFind meaning & patternsConvey meaning & persuade
SkillPattern-spotting · analysisClarity · tone · structure
Use caseMedia research · campaign evalSpeaking · writing · persuasion

Importance of content analysis

  • Understand audience behaviour — how people interact with content
  • Evaluate marketing effectiveness — measure campaign impact
  • Identify trends & patterns — surface preferences, shifts in opinion
  • Inform strategic decisions — feed insights into marketing, PR, policy

Scope — where it's used

  • Media studies — news articles, social media, advertising
  • Marketing research — brand reputation, consumer behaviour
  • Social sciences — public opinion, attitudes
  • Policy analysis — evaluate policy docs & effectiveness

Importance of articulation

  • Effective communication — clear expression, no ambiguity
  • Builds relationships — empathy, mutual understanding
  • Persuasion & influence — compelling delivery convinces
  • Personal/professional growth — confidence to speak up

Scope of articulation

  • Public speaking · writing/editing · marketing/advertising · education/training

4 key aspects (always check)

  1. Clarity — ideas easy to understand
  2. Accuracy — content matches intended message
  3. Context — fits situation/setting
  4. Audience — tailored to listener's level

7-step compression of an article's core ideas

  1. Read carefully · understand
  2. Identify main ideas (headings, bullets)
  3. Determine core message / thesis
  4. Remove non-essential info (anecdotes)
  5. Condense into bullet points
  6. Clear language · no jargon
  7. Review & refine
Mnemonic: "RIDRCCR" — Read · Identify · Determine · Remove · Condense · Clear · Review.

Why compress core ideas? (5 reasons)

  • Improves understanding — simplifies complex info, enhances comprehension
  • Saves time — readers grasp fast, productivity ↑
  • Enhances retention — concise = memorable
  • Facilitates sharing — short summaries spread fast, spark discussion
  • Supports decisions — key insights → informed choice

2.2 Identifying Credible Sources 4 M

Scoring: Def (0.5) + 4 factors with example (2) + things to avoid (0.5) + importance (0.5) + consequences (0.5) = 4 M
Credible source Source whose info is accurate, reliable & trustworthy — backed by author authority, peer-review, or established institution.

4 factors — the "APPA" check

tbl 2.2 · 4 credibility factors
FactorWhat to verifyExamples
AuthorityAuthor credentials · institutional affiliation · peer-reviewed?JAMA · Nature · Stanford prof
PurposeInform / educate / persuade / sell? Objective or biased?News article vs sponsored post
PublicationReputable journal / academic press / govt source?Science journal · NIH report · NYT · BBC
AccuracyUp-to-date · fact-checked · references provided?WHO datasheet w/ citations

Examples of credible sources

  • Academic journals — JAMA, Nature, Science
  • Government reports — NIH, CDC, WHO
  • Reputable news — NYT, BBC, NPR
  • Academic books — university press, reputable publishers

Things to avoid

  • Unknown / anonymous authors
  • Biased loaded language
  • Outdated info (5+ yr in fast fields)
  • No references / no citations

Why it matters

Importance Academic integrity · avoids plagiarism, supports arguments · research quality — accurate, valid · informed decisions — avoids misinformation · professional credibility — proves expertise.

Consequences of using non-credible sources

  • Loss of credibility — reputation hit
  • Inaccurate info — leads to wrong conclusions
  • Plagiarism / academic dishonesty — citing fake sources

2.3 CRAAP Test 8 M

Scoring: Acronym + def (1) + 5 letters with key Q (5) + example for each (1) + why it matters (1) = 8 M
CRAAP test Checklist of 5 criteria to evaluate any source before citing it. Stops "internet says so" thinking → forces critical evaluation.
C
CurrencyIs info up-to-date? When published / updated? Working links?
Eg: 2010 cancer-treatment article ≠ usable today.
R
RelevanceDoes it match my topic + audience level? Too basic / advanced?
Eg: kids' encyclopaedia for a research paper = irrelevant.
A
AuthorityWho wrote? Credentials? Institution? Contact info?
Eg: NASA scientist on climate > random blogger.
A
AccuracyEvidence-based? Citations? Verifiable elsewhere? Spelling/grammar?
Eg: stat from peer-reviewed journal = accurate.
P
PurposeInform / teach / sell / persuade / entertain? Bias visible?
Eg: ad disguised as news article = purpose to sell.
Mnemonic: Garbage source = "CRAAP" you don't want. Test rejects crap.
Q-style "How can you determine whether a source is credible? Discuss CRAAP — currency, relevance, authority, accuracy, purpose with examples." (10-M Part-B). → Always lead with the diagram + 1 example per letter.

2.4 Bias & Eliminating Bias 4 M

Bias Systematic tilt away from objective truth — caused by personal beliefs, agenda, or missing perspective. Distorts how arguments are built and judged.

How bias affects argument accuracy (4 ways)

  • Cherry-picks evidence — picks data that confirms, ignores rest
  • Distorts tone — loaded language ("greedy corporations") cues emotion over logic
  • Hides assumptions — sneaks unstated premises into the conclusion
  • Misleads audience — even true facts → wrong conclusion if framed unfairly

6 strategies to identify & eliminate bias

tbl 2.4 · bias-elimination toolkit
StepWhat to do
1. Spot loaded languageReplace emotive words with neutral ones
2. Cross-check sourcesUse 2+ credible sources with opposing slants
3. Check funding / agendaWho paid? What do they gain?
4. Read opposing viewRead the strongest counter-argument
5. Apply CRAAPAuthority + Purpose flags bias fast
6. Use diverse sampleFor data — broaden demographics

PYQ Q6(a) [5-M]: "What is bias in content analysis · how does it impact arguments · strategies to identify and eliminate." → def + 4 distortion modes + 6-step table + diagram.

2.5 Evaluating Arguments & Alternative Viewpoints 4 / 5 M

Argument evaluation Process of assessing claims, evidence & reasoning to decide if an argument is valid, sound & well-supported.

Evaluate & respond — 4-step pipe

  1. Identify claim — what is being argued?
  2. Assess evidence — strong? credible? sufficient?
  3. Consider counterargument — opposing view, weaknesses
  4. Respond thoughtfully — well-reasoned reply

Test hypotheses against criteria — 4 steps

  1. Formulate hypothesis based on available info
  2. Identify relevant criteria to evaluate it
  3. Gather evidence to support / refute
  4. Evaluate hypothesis against criteria + evidence

Analyze information & form judgment — 4 steps

  1. Break down complex info into smaller parts
  2. Identify patterns & relationships
  3. Evaluate credibility of each source
  4. Form well-supported judgment

Why assess alternative viewpoints? (5 reasons)

  • Cuts confirmation bias — exposes blind spots in own thinking
  • Strengthens own argument — anticipates counters, pre-empts them
  • Better decisions — multi-perspective view → informed choice
  • Builds empathy — understanding "why others think differently"
  • Innovation — new ideas come from clashing viewpoints

Assess viewpoints — 4-step process

  1. Consider multiple perspectives on the issue
  2. Evaluate strengths & weaknesses of each
  3. Identify biases / assumptions in each
  4. Synthesize info from all → well-rounded view
PYQ Q1(f) [1.5-M] / Q5(a) [5-M]: "Why important to assess alternative viewpoints?" → 5 reasons + 4-step process + 1 example (e.g. team designing app considers non-tech user view).

3.1 Interview — concept & types 4 M

Interview Structured two-way conversation where interviewer assesses candidate's skills, fit & communication for a role/admission.

Types

  • HR / behavioural — past actions predict future · STAR-friendly
  • Technical — domain knowledge, problem-solving
  • Panel — multiple interviewers, group setting
  • Stress — checks composure under pressure
  • Telephonic / video — first-round filter

Why interviews matter

  • Tests communication + reasoning under time pressure
  • Reveals cultural fit beyond a resume
  • Two-way — candidate also evaluates the company

3.2 STAR Method 15 M

Scoring (15-M): Def + acronym (1) · 4 steps with role (6) · diagram (2) · worked example (4) · why useful — 5 pts (2) = 15 M
STAR Method 4-step framework — Situation · Task · Action · Result — to answer behavioural interview questions in a structured, evidence-rich way.
SSituationset context
TTaskyour goal
AActionwhat YOU did
RResultoutcome+lesson

Role of each step

tbl 3.2 · STAR step-by-step
StepWhat to giveWhy it matters
SituationWhen · where · context (1-2 sentences)Anchors the story · interviewer can visualise
TaskYour responsibility · the challenge / goalShows the "stakes" · what you owned
ActionSpecific steps YOU took (use "I", not "we")Most-weighted · proves your skill, decision & effort
ResultOutcome · numbers · lesson learntQuantifies impact · ends story strong

Worked example — "Tell me about a time you led a team."

STAR answer S: 3rd-year college fest, our 5-member team had to deliver a coding workshop in 2 weeks.
T: I was lead — had to plan, assign work, run the workshop for 60 students.
A: Split team by skill (content / promo / logistics), set daily 10-min standups, built backup plan when 1 member dropped, ran a dry-run 2 days before.
R: Workshop ran on time · 58 / 60 attendance · 4.6/5 feedback. Learnt — early dry-run catches 80% of issues.
Mnemonic: "STAR — Set · Tell · Act · Reveal".

Why STAR is useful (5 reasons)

  • Structure — stops rambling · ~2-min answer
  • Evidence — facts + numbers prove the skill, not just claims
  • Memorable — story format sticks better than abstract
  • Confidence — interviewer-friendly · candidate stays calm
  • Universal — works for HR / leadership / failure / conflict Qs
Pro tip — always end with a number ("4.6/5", "saved 30%", "60 attendees"). Numbers anchor results.

3.3 Mock Interview — Benefits, Roles, Significance 10 M

Scoring (10-M): Def (1) · 6 benefits (4) · 3 roles (2) · 4 significance pts (2) · 1 example (1) = 10 M
Mock interview Practice / simulated interview — friend, mentor or coach plays interviewer. Run in real conditions to build skill before the real thing.

6 benefits

  • Confidence — kills first-time nerves
  • Communication — fixes filler words ("umm"), pace, clarity
  • Feedback loop — instant correction on body language, voice, content
  • Problem-solving practice — handles unexpected Qs in safe space
  • Identifies gaps — weak topics surface before real interview
  • Reduces anxiety — familiarises the format

3 roles

tbl 3.3 · mock interview roles
RoleJob
CandidateAnswer Qs · stay in role · take feedback
InterviewerAsk realistic Qs · stay neutral · note gaps
Observer / coachWatch body language · timing · give detailed feedback

4 significance points (why it works)

  • Safe failure — make mistakes without losing the offer
  • Repetition — multiple mocks build muscle memory of STAR answers
  • Self-awareness — recordings reveal habits you don't see otherwise
  • Targeted growth — feedback shows exactly what to fix
add example — "Did 3 mocks before campus drive · improved my STAR delivery from 5 min ramble to clean 90 sec."

4.1 Conflict & Resolution Strategies 10 M

Scoring (10-M): Def (1) · 3 P's with role (3) · 5 strategies + when to use (4) · diagram (1) · example (1) = 10 M
Conflict Disagreement / clash between two or more parties over interests, values or needs.
Conflict resolution Process of finding a peaceful solution that meets needs of all parties — combines communication, negotiation, compromise & problem-solving.

4 key elements of conflict resolution

  • Communication — clear expression of needs
  • Negotiation — work together toward acceptable solution
  • Compromise — mutual concessions
  • Problem-solving — identify & address root issues

3 P's of conflict resolution

tbl 4.1 · the 3 P's
PMeaningHow it helps
ProblemIdentify root cause — not symptomStops fighting wrong target
PeopleUnderstand perspectives & needs of each partyBuilds empathy · separates person from problem
ProcessDefine steps — communication, negotiation, compromiseEnsures fair, structured path

8 strategies to resolve conflict (action playbook)

  1. Active listening — listen carefully, respond thoughtfully
  2. Stay calm & composed — manage emotions to avoid escalation
  3. Focus on interests, not positions — understand why they want X
  4. Seek common ground — find areas of agreement
  5. Communicate effectively — clearly express thoughts, feelings, needs
  6. Use non-confrontational language — avoid blame / attack words
  7. Seek mediation / arbitration — neutral third party if stuck
  8. Be open to compromise — find middle ground

5 conflict-resolution strategies (Thomas-Kilmann)

tbl 4.2 · 5 strategies
StrategyStanceWhen to useExample
AvoidWithdraw · sidestepTrivial issue · need cool-offIgnore minor seating tiff
CompromiseEach side gives someEqual power · time crunchSplit shared budget 50-50
AccommodateYield to otherOther's need urgent · keep relationLet teammate pick topic this time
CompeteWin at any costCrisis · safety · ethics issueManager halts unsafe shortcut
CollaborateWin-win · joint solveImportant issue · time availableBrainstorm to merge two designs
Mnemonic: "A Cat Always Catches Cream" → Avoid · Compromise · Accommodate · Compete · Collaborate.

How to choose the right strategy — 4 factors

  • Importance of the issue
  • Relationship between parties
  • Trust & communication level
  • Consequences of each strategy

Benefits / drawbacks (quick balance)

  • Collaborate → creative solutions, but time-consuming
  • Compete → effective for assertion, but damages relationships
  • Avoid → cools temper, but doesn't solve root cause

Importance of conflict resolution

  • Personal relationships — strengthens bonds
  • Workplace productivity — unblocked teams ship more
  • Social harmony — reduces tensions in community

4.2 Group Discussion — basics 4 / 5 M

GD Group of 6-12 people discuss a topic for 10-15 min. Used to assess critical thinking, communication, teamwork, leadership.

4 evaluation pillars (key elements)

tbl 4.3 · GD evaluation pillars
PillarWhat evaluator checks
CommunicationClarity · vocab · listening
Critical thinkingAnalysing topic · logical args · counter-args
TeamworkBuilding on others · respectful disagreement
LeadershipInitiating · guiding · summarising

3 objectives

  • Idea generation — pool of solutions
  • Problem-solving — collective brainpower
  • Decision / consensus — group reaches a stand

3 benefits

  • Improved communication — develop expression skills
  • Enhanced critical thinking — analyse complex issues
  • Team building — collaboration & respect

Topic tiers (regional → international)

  • Regional — water scarcity (India) · traffic congestion · regional disparities
  • National — economic growth · education reform · national security
  • International — climate change · global inequality · pandemics

4 GD exercise types

  • Case study analysis — analyse real / chronic problem
  • Policy debate — debate merits of different approaches
  • Solution generation — brainstorm fixes for a problem
  • Stakeholder analysis — view problem through different parties

Tips to score in a GD

  • Initiate if confident — first speaker scores high if structured
  • Listen · build on previous point ("agreeing with X, I'd add…")
  • Don't shout · respect turn-taking
  • Summarise at end if group wanders
  • Use data + examples, not just opinions
avoid — interrupting · "I disagree completely" without reason · staying silent > 2 min · contradicting yourself.
