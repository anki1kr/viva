Bluetooth, Piconets & Scatternets ⭐ Repeated 3×

Frequency Reuse & Cluster Math ⭐ Guaranteed 10M

Frequency Reuse Low-power antennas allow the same radio channels to be reused in faraway cells. Nearby cells are grouped into a Cluster (N). Each cell inside a cluster gets a distinct set of channels.
Frequency Reuse Formulas
• Q = D / R = sqrt(3N) (Co-channel reuse ratio)
• Standard N sizes = i² + ij + j² (Common sizes: 3, 4, 7, 9, 12)

A smaller cluster size N increases overall network capacity but reduces the physical distance between co-channel cells, increasing Co-channel Interference (CCI).

Unit III

03Multiple Access &
Spread Spectrum

Sharing the sky safely using time slots, frequency slots, and unique code mathematics.

Multiple Access: FDMA, TDMA, CDMA, SDMA ⭐ Repeated 4×

Access Schemes Mnemonic • FDMA: Separate users by frequency (one channel per user).
• TDMA: Separate users by time slots (all share one frequency in turns).
• CDMA: Separate users by orthogonal mathematical codes (all share same band simultaneously).
• SDMA: Separate users by pointing narrow spatial antenna beams.
Near-Far Problem in CDMA If a mobile user is physically close to the base station, its signal will completely drown out distant users on the same frequency. CDMA resolves this by enforcing Strict Power Control loops (checking and adjusting mobile power 800 times a second).

Spread Spectrum: DSSS vs FHSS ⭐ Repeated 2×

Spread Spectrum (SS) Intentionally spreading a narrow-band data signal across a much wider radio frequency band. This reduces interference susceptibility and enables anti-jamming.
  • DSSS (Direct Sequence): Multiplies data bits by a fast Pseudo-Noise (PN) chip code. Low probability of intercept. Used in GPS.
  • FHSS (Frequency Hopping): Carrier frequency hops rapidly across many channels according to a PN sequence. Outstanding anti-interference. Used in Bluetooth.
Unit IV

04Wireless Networking &
Routing Architectures

Network routing, ISDN architectural channels, and intelligent systems.

ISDN & AIN Architectures

  • ISDN (Integrated Services Digital Network): Integrates voice and data over standard phone lines. Channels: B Channel (Bearer, 64 kbps, carries voice/data) and D Channel (Delta, 16 kbps, handles out-of-band signaling).
  • AIN (Advanced Intelligent Network): Decouples call-routing databases from physical switches. Allows rapid deployment of custom services (toll-free numbers, call forwarding) without updating local exchange software.

Q2Unit 1 Highlight15M
Describe the GSM System Architecture in detail with a neat block diagram. Detail the role of Mobile Station (MS), Base Station Subsystem (BSS), and Network Switching Subsystem (NSS) with a table.
Introduction

GSM (Global System for Mobile communications) is the canonical second-generation digital cellular standard. The full architecture decouples the system into four functional subsystems (Mobile Station, Base Station Subsystem, Network Switching Subsystem, and Operations Support Subsystem) connected by four standard interfaces (Um, Abis, A, and the SS7-family interfaces inside the NSS). This clean layering enables multi-vendor hardware compatibility, seamless inter-operator roaming, and the orderly evolution to 3G/4G/5G. GSM operates in three primary frequency bands:

  • GSM-900: uplink 890–915 MHz, downlink 935–960 MHz.
  • DCS-1800: 1710–1785 / 1805–1880 MHz (Europe / Asia).
  • PCS-1900: 1850–1910 / 1930–1990 MHz (North America).

Each carrier is 200 kHz wide and supports 8 TDMA full-rate timeslots.

1 · Mobile Station (MS) — the subscriber endpoint
  • Mobile Equipment (ME): the physical handset (RF transceiver, baseband DSP, vocoder, display, keypad, battery). Identified uniquely by the IMEI (International Mobile Equipment Identity, 15 digits) burned into firmware at manufacture; checked against the EIR before granting service.
  • SIM (Subscriber Identity Module): smart-card holding the IMSI (International Mobile Subscriber Identity, max 15 digits: 3-digit MCC + 2/3-digit MNC + MSIN), the secret authentication key Ki, the temporary identity TMSI, the cipher key Kc after authentication, plus phonebook/SMS storage. The SIM decouples identity from hardware: swap SIMs, keep the same number.
  • MSISDN (Mobile Station ISDN Number) is the public dialable number associated with the subscription in the HLR; the IMSI is private and never travels over the air in cleartext after first attach (TMSI replaces it).
  • Power classes: handhelds typically Class 4 (2 W @ 900 MHz) or Class 1 (1 W @ 1800 MHz); transmit power is dynamically adjusted by the BSC in 2 dB steps to minimise interference and conserve battery.
2 · Base Station Subsystem (BSS) — the radio access layer
  • BTS (Base Transceiver Station): the radio tower hardware (antennas, TRX units per carrier, power amplifiers, combiners, climate control). Handles the Um air interface, performs GMSK modulation/demodulation, channel coding/decoding, ciphering A5/1, frequency hopping, and the timing-advance loop.
  • BSC (Base Station Controller): the intelligence head controlling typically 10–100 BTS sites over the Abis interface. Allocates radio channels, runs the handover decision algorithm (intra-BSC handovers are entirely local and never reach the MSC), manages power control commands, and concentrates the BTS traffic over the A interface to the MSC.
  • TRAU (Transcoder & Rate Adaptation Unit): often physically inside the BSC or MSC; converts the GSM full-rate 13 kbps speech codec into the 64 kbps PCM used inside the wired PSTN.
  • Cell types served: macrocells (1–35 km), microcells (≤ 1 km), picocells (indoor, ≤ 100 m). The BSS treats all transparently; only antenna power and timing-advance window differ.
3 · Network Switching Subsystem (NSS) — the core network
  • MSC (Mobile Services Switching Centre): the heart of the NSS, a circuit-switched exchange that sets up, routes, and tears down calls. Performs all PSTN-facing signalling via SS7, handles inter-BSC and inter-MSC handovers, and orchestrates billing CDRs. A GMSC (Gateway MSC) is the specific MSC that interfaces with external PSTN/PLMN networks for incoming roamer calls.
  • HLR (Home Location Register): the permanent master database of every subscriber on the operator's network — one logical HLR per PLMN. Stores the IMSI, MSISDN, Ki (reference copy), service profile, supplementary services, and a pointer to the current serving VLR. Updated only at subscription provisioning or location-area change.
  • VLR (Visitor Location Register): a volatile local cache co-located with each MSC, holding a partial copy of every active subscriber's HLR record currently in the MSC's service area. Cuts cross-network HLR queries from millions/hour to once-per-location-update.
  • AuC (Authentication Centre): a tamper-proof database, paired with the HLR, that produces the authentication triplet (RAND, SRES, Kc) using the A3 (signed-response) and A8 (cipher-key) algorithms keyed by the subscriber's secret Ki. The triplet is delivered to the VLR over the D interface; the MS computes its own SRES from RAND with its own Ki, and the VLR compares.
  • EIR (Equipment Identity Register): three lists keyed by IMEI: white (allowed), grey (tracked but allowed), black (stolen, service denied). Cross-checked at every attach.
4 · Operations Support Subsystem (OSS) — manage & maintain
  • NMC (Network Management Centre): top-tier, network-wide dashboard for the entire PLMN — strategic planning, performance dashboards, security policy.
  • OMC (Operations & Maintenance Centre): regional / per-BSS layer handling the classic FCAPS functions: Fault management (alarms, trouble tickets), Configuration management (cell-plan changes, software upgrades), Accounting (CDR collection, mediation to the billing system), Performance management (KPIs: drop rate, handover success, RxLev distribution), Security management (audit logs, key rotation).
  • Connected to BSS and NSS via TCP/IP-based Q3 (TMN) interfaces, typically out-of-band on a separate management network.
  • OSS is the only subsystem a human operator interacts with daily; the rest run autonomously.

Worked Example — Outgoing Call Setup
  1. User dials a number; MS sends Channel Request on RACH (Um interface).
  2. BTS assigns SDCCH; MS sends Service Request with TMSI; BSC forwards to MSC over A interface.
  3. MSC asks VLR for the triplet (RAND, SRES, Kc); VLR replies (or fetches from AuC via D interface if cache miss).
  4. BTS sends RAND to MS over the air; MS computes SRES locally using Ki and A3; returns SRES.
  5. VLR compares: match? MS then computes Kc with A8, ciphering starts on the air interface.
  6. MSC routes the dialled number to the GMSC, which forwards over SS7 to the PSTN, and the call rings on the called party's side.
Examiner Trap — HLR vs VLR & IMSI vs IMEI Two classic confusions cost 3–4 marks: (1) HLR is the permanent per-operator database (one logical instance, replicated for resilience); VLR is the volatile local cache co-located with each MSC. (2) IMSI identifies the subscriber (lives on the SIM); IMEI identifies the handset (lives in firmware). The EIR uses IMEI to ban stolen phones; the HLR uses IMSI to authenticate the subscription. Get either pair wrong and the examiner deducts.
Modern Relevance

Although 5G NR is the headline 2026 wireless standard, the GSM (2G) architecture remains the universal voice fallback layer. Major carriers (Vodafone, Airtel, T-Mobile) continue operating GSM cores for CSFB (Circuit-Switched Fallback) and IoT M2M devices that use GSM/GPRS modems for low-cost telemetry. The clean MS / BSS / NSS / OSS partition seeded every subsequent generation: UMTS introduced RNC + Node-B (a BSS variant) and the same HLR/AuC; LTE replaced MSC/HLR with the all-IP MME + HSS + SGW/PGW in the EPC; 5G introduced the Service-Based Architecture with AMF/SMF/UDM serving the same functions. The decoupling principle still rules cellular design.

Conclusion

GSM's four-subsystem layered architecture is the textbook reference for cellular system design. Clean separation between handset (MS), radio access (BSS), switching (NSS), and operations (OSS), bound by standardised Um, Abis, A, and SS7-family interfaces, enables vendor interoperability, seamless mobility, and international roaming. Mastering the MS/BSS/NSS/OSS partition, the IMSI/IMEI/TMSI/MSISDN identifier set, the AuC authentication triplet, and the role of each NSS element (MSC, HLR, VLR, AuC, EIR) unlocks every subsequent generation question (UMTS, LTE EPC, 5G SBA).

Q3Unit 2 Numerical15M
A total bandwidth of 25 MHz is allocated to a cellular system with N=7 frequency reuse cluster. If each simplex channel is 25 kHz (duplex pair = 50 kHz), and 1 MHz is reserved for control channels:
(a) Find the total duplex channels in the system.
(b) Compute voice & control channels per cell.
(c) If cell radius R = 2 km, find co-channel reuse ratio Q and co-channel cell distance D.
Introduction

Frequency reuse is the cornerstone of cellular capacity: the same set of radio channels is spatially re-deployed in cells that are separated by a safe distance, organised into a repeating geometric cluster size N. The hexagonal cell tessellation constrains the allowed cluster sizes to those satisfying N = i² + ij + j² for non-negative integers i, j — giving the canonical set N ∈ {1, 3, 4, 7, 9, 12, 13, 19, 21 …}. Smaller N packs more frequency repetition per area (higher capacity) but tightens the co-channel distance and degrades the S/I ratio.

The Co-Channel Distance Formula — Quick Derivation
Step
For a hexagonal cell of inscribed (cell) radius R, the centre-to-centre spacing between adjacent cells is R√3. Moving i cells along one axis and then j cells along the 60°-rotated axis lands on the nearest co-channel cell, separated by D = R√(3N). Therefore the dimensionless co-channel reuse ratio is Q = D / R = √(3N). Larger N ⇒ larger Q ⇒ better S/I but lower capacity.
(a) Total Duplex Channels
Step 1
Compute duplex channel width: each call requires one uplink and one downlink simplex channel. Duplex size = 2 × 25 kHz = 50 kHz = 0.05 MHz.
Step 2
Compute total system duplex channels: Total Channels = 25 MHz / 0.05 MHz = 500 duplex channels.
(b) Voice & Control Channels per Cell
Step 3
Compute system-wide control channels from reserved bandwidth: 1 MHz / 0.05 MHz = 20 control channels.
Step 4
Compute system-wide voice channels: 500 total − 20 control = 480 voice channels.
Step 5
Compute voice and control channels per cell (using cluster size N = 7):
• Voice channels per cell = 480 / 7 ≈ 68.57 → 68 channels per cell (rounded down).
• Control channels per cell = 20 / 7 ≈ 2.86 → 3 channels per cell (rounded up).
(c) Reuse Ratio Q & Co-channel Distance D
Step 6
Compute co-channel reuse ratio Q: Q = sqrt(3 N) = sqrt(21) ≈ 4.58.
Step 7
Compute co-channel cell distance D (using cell radius R = 2 km): D = Q × R = 4.58 × 2 km = 9.16 km.

Boxed answer: (a) 500 duplex channels, (b) 68 voice + 3 control channels per cell, (c) Q ≈ 4.58, D ≈ 9.16 km.

Sanity Check — Second Scenario (N = 4 Cluster, Smaller Spectrum)

To prove the formulas scale: re-do with B = 1.5 MHz total, N = 4 cluster, simplex channel 10 kHz (duplex 20 kHz), control overhead 60 kHz, cell radius R = 1.5 km.

Step A
Total duplex channels = 1500 kHz / 20 kHz = 75 channels. Control channels = 60/20 = 3. Voice channels = 75 − 3 = 72.
Step B
Voice per cell = 72 / 4 = 18 channels. Control per cell = ⌈3/4⌉ = 1 channel.
Step C
Q = √(3 × 4) = √12 ≈ 3.46; D = 3.46 × 1.5 = ≈ 5.2 km. (Smaller N ⇒ tighter Q ⇒ shorter co-channel distance.)

Examiner Trap — D/R factor & rounding The most common mis-step is computing D = √(3N) without the × R factor — Q is dimensionless (it is the ratio D/R), but the actual co-channel distance D = Q × R has units of km. Drop the multiplication and you lose 1.5–2 marks. Also: when channels-per-cell is not an integer, voice channels round down (no half-channel for voice — wastes the fraction) and control channels round up (you cannot have fractional signalling — every cell must have at least one CCH). And always remember that one duplex channel = 2 simplex carriers — half the students forget the ×2.
Modern Relevance

Classic N = 7 reuse was the 2G GSM staple and still appears on cellular planning slides; 4G LTE and 5G NR moved to universal reuse (N = 1) with Fractional Frequency Reuse (FFR) and Soft Frequency Reuse (SFR): interior cell users receive the full band while edge users are restricted to orthogonal sub-bands to control co-channel interference. The classical hexagonal cluster geometry survives inside modern RF planning tools (Atoll, ICS Telecom, RANplan), and is the conceptual basis of Coordinated Multi-Point (CoMP) transmission and 5G beam-based interference coordination.

Conclusion

Frequency reuse remains the single most powerful capacity multiplier in any spatially-bounded radio system: halving the cluster size roughly doubles the per-cell channel count, but the cost is a smaller Q, a smaller co-channel distance, and a degraded S/I. Cellular operators trade these knobs against subscriber density, antenna heights, and modulation robustness. Modern networks push the principle to its physical limit — universal reuse plus aggressive interference coordination — but the geometry derived above (N = i² + ij + j², D = R√(3N)) still governs the cell plan.

Q4Unit 2 Highlight15M
Compare MAHO, MCHO, and NCHO handoff strategies. Discuss the practical handoff considerations: hysteresis, dwell time, guard channels, and the ping-pong effect. Trace the 4-stage handoff sequence.
Introduction

A handoff (handover) transfers an active call from one radio channel or base station to another without dropping the user session, as the subscriber moves across a cell boundary or as link conditions degrade. Without robust handoff, cellular geometry would be useless: every cell crossing would terminate a call. The control logic has evolved through three architectural strategies (NCHO → MAHO → MCHO), distinguished by who measures the radio link and who makes the switching decision. In parallel, the radio-side classification splits into hard, soft, and softer handoffs based on how the transition is executed on the air interface.

NCHO — Network-Controlled Handoff
  • Who measures: The base station continuously samples the mobile's uplink signal strength and SNR for its own cell, and (via dedicated locator receivers) for neighbouring cells too.
  • Who decides: Decision is centralised at the MSC. The MSC collects measurement reports from many BSs, identifies a better candidate, and instructs the handover.
  • Response time: Very slow, typically 5–10 seconds between trigger condition and actual switching. Sufficient for low-mobility vehicular traffic but useless for fast walking pedestrians inside microcells.
  • Signalling load: Heavy on the BS↔MSC backhaul; the mobile is completely passive: it neither measures nor decides anything.
  • Standard: 1G analog systems (AMPS, TACS, NMT). Phased out as digital cellular matured.
MAHO — Mobile-Assisted Handoff
  • Who measures: The mobile phone itself, during idle TDMA slots, continuously scans the BCCH carriers of up to 6 neighbouring cells (the BA-list, Neighbour Cell List) and measures their RxLev / RxQual.
  • Who decides: Mobile periodically reports its measurement set (every SACCH frame ≈ 480 ms in GSM) to the serving BSC; the BSC + MSC evaluate the ranking and trigger the handover.
  • Response time: Fast, typically 1–2 seconds; well-suited to vehicular speeds (≤ 120 km/h) and most pedestrian traffic.
  • Network entities involved: MS measures, BTS forwards measurement reports, BSC runs the handover algorithm (intra-BSC handovers are handled locally), MSC coordinates only inter-BSC and inter-MSC cases.
  • Standard: 2G GSM, IS-136 (D-AMPS), 3G UMTS. The dominant strategy in deployed cellular networks.
MCHO — Mobile-Controlled Handoff
  • Who measures: Mobile phone. Same as MAHO.
  • Who decides: The mobile itself picks the target and initiates the switching; the network is informed after-the-fact and merely re-routes traffic.
  • Response time: Near-instant, under 100 ms; ideal for private indoor systems where cell boundaries are crossed frequently inside a single office floor.
  • Signalling load: Minimal on the backbone — most logic is on the mobile side. Trade-off: each handset is more complex and battery hungry.
  • Standard: DECT (cordless), early Wi-Fi roaming, private Bluetooth networks. Not used in macro-cellular networks because operators want to keep handover policy under their own control (anti-poaching across operators).

Practical Handoff Considerations
  • Hysteresis margin: The candidate cell's RxLev must exceed the serving cell's by a fixed margin (typically 3–6 dB in GSM) before the handover is triggered. Without it, brief Rayleigh fades would constantly toggle the active cell.
  • Dwell time: The continuous duration the candidate must stay better-than-threshold (typically ≥ 5 seconds). Filters out transient spikes from constructive multipath or open windows in moving vehicles.
  • Guard channels: A fixed pool of channels in every cell reserved for incoming handovers and unavailable to new originating calls. Trades blocking probability for new calls (Bnew ↑) against drop probability for handovers (Pdrop ↓); a 3 % guard reserve typically suffices.
  • Ping-pong effect: Rapid, repetitive back-and-forth handovers at a sharp cell boundary caused by fading or geometry. Eliminated by combining hysteresis + dwell time: the candidate must be both better and persistently better. Also mitigated by directional retry in 3G (mobile sticks to last serving cell unless candidate is significantly better).
  • Cell-dragging: A pedestrian with a strong line-of-sight signal can stay attached to a distant cell long after geometrically it should have switched, raising co-channel interference for everyone else. Combatted by an absolute RxLev floor below which handover is forced regardless of relative comparison.
The 4-Stage Handoff Sequence
1
Measure Phase: MS continuously measures downlink signal strengths (RSS) from neighbouring base stations and reports to current BSC.
2
Decide Phase: BSC detects that RSS falls below threshold (e.g. -100 dBm) and hysteresis is met. Initiates handoff request to MSC.
3
Execute Phase: MSC locates new target BS, reserves a traffic channel, and instructs current BS to send a handover command to MS.
4
Complete Phase: MS retunes to the new frequency, establishes link with the new BS, and old BS releases the previous channel back to the pool.
Examiner Trap — MAHO vs NCHO The classic confusion: NCHO uses base station measurements (mobile is dumb, network is smart) — used in slow 1G AMPS. MAHO uses mobile measurements but the MSC still decides — used in 2G GSM. Don't merge the two; an examiner who reads "mobile measures and decides" for MAHO marks it wrong (that's MCHO, used in DECT).
Modern Relevance

4G LTE and 5G NR replace classical hard handover with soft handover (CDMA2000/UMTS) and make-before-break X2/Xn handover (LTE/NR), where the mobile is briefly connected to both source and target cells — eliminating call drops. Future 6G architectures (3GPP Rel-19+) target zero-interruption mobility using ML-driven handover prediction.

Conclusion

Handoff sits at the heart of cellular mobility — the trade-off between measurement accuracy, decision latency, and signalling overhead has driven the entire evolution from NCHO → MAHO → MCHO → soft handover. Coupled with hysteresis, dwell time, and guard channels, modern networks achieve sub-50 ms break times.

Contrast Cell Splitting, Cell Sectoring, and Microzone cell designs in detail. Explain how each increases coverage or capacity, with a detailed comparison table.
Introduction

As cellular subscriber density grows, operators must scale capacity without buying expensive additional radio spectrum. Three classic architectural techniques solve this — cell splitting (geometric subdivision), sectoring (directional antennas), and microcell zone deployment (distributed low-power zones). They form the textbook capacity-extension toolkit; each trades capital cost, handover signalling load, and interference geometry differently.

1 · Cell Splitting — Geometric Subdivision
  • Mechanism: A congested cell of radius R is subdivided into four smaller cells each of radius R/2, with new BTS sites at the new centres. Original transmit power is reduced by a factor of 16 (path-loss with n = 4) to preserve the same coverage geometry.
  • Capacity formula: Since cell area scales as R², halving the radius yields (R/R/2)² = 4× more cells per km² — and therefore capacity ×4 more channels per km². The cluster size N is preserved, so S/I is unchanged.
  • Worked numerical: A macro cell of R = 10 km serving 100 channels supports 1 channel/km² in its 314 km² footprint. Splitting to R/2 = 5 km gives 4 cells × 100 channels in the same 314 km² → 1.27 channels/km² ⇒ raw capacity 4×.
  • Drawback: Capital intensive: each split needs new tower, backhaul, and site acquisition. Handover signalling load jumps roughly 4× because users now cross more boundaries per minute at the same walking/driving speed.
  • Used when: A specific cell is consistently congested (Erlang-B blocking > 2 %) and physical space for additional sites exists. The "umbrella cell" technique keeps the original large cell active at reduced power for high-speed users, while the new small cells serve slow / stationary traffic.
2 · Cell Sectoring — Directional Antennas
  • Mechanism: The single omni-directional antenna at the cell centre is replaced by 3 directional antennas at 120° (or 6 at 60°), each illuminating its own pie-slice sector and reusing only a 1/3 (or 1/6) subset of the cell's channels.
  • Interference reduction: With 3-sector 120° antennas, the number of dominant first-tier interferers drops from 6 to 2; with 6-sector, it drops to 1. Therefore S/I improves by ~4.7 dB (3-sector) or ~7.8 dB (6-sector) over omni.
  • Enables smaller cluster: The improved S/I allows the operator to drop the cluster size from N = 7 → N = 4 or N = 3 while still meeting the 18 dB threshold, raising frequency-reuse density and effective capacity by (7/4) ≈ 1.75× without any extra hardware beyond the antenna swap.
  • Capital cost: Very low: only the antenna head is replaced; the BTS cabinet, backhaul, and licences are untouched. The single cheapest capacity upgrade.
  • Drawback: Trunking efficiency drops; splitting 60 cell-wide channels into 3 sector pools of 20 each means each sector's Erlang-B blocking is worse for the same offered load (smaller pools waste more channels through randomness). Also, sector boundary crossings inside the same cell trigger intra-cell sector handovers: extra signalling.
3 · Microcell zone — Distributed Antennas, One BTS
  • Mechanism: Multiple low-power "zone" antennas (Distributed Antenna System, DAS) are installed throughout one large cell (under street lights, building rooftops, lamp posts), all fed by a single central base station through optical fibre. Each antenna covers a small zone of ~100 m radius.
  • Single-BS architecture: The user's call is dynamically switched from one zone antenna to another as the user moves, but it stays in the same BTS, so the handover is internal and never reaches the BSC. Result: zero classical handovers across the entire macro cell.
  • Capacity gain: Each zone has its own subset of channels, effectively delivering small-cell capacity over the original macro footprint. Achieves capacity densities comparable to splitting but without the multi-BTS site cost.
  • Worked numerical: A 1-km macro cell with 60 channels traditionally serves 60 simultaneous calls. Replacing it with 9 zone antennas in a 3×3 grid (each with 60 channels via spatial reuse inside the cell) raises effective capacity to ~9 × 60 = 540 channels in the same macro footprint, at the cost of fibre/cabling, not towers.
  • Drawback: Fibre / coaxial cabling between zones and central BTS is expensive to retrofit in legacy buildings. Best suited to greenfield campuses, airports, malls, stadiums where conduit is already planned.

Trunking & Handoff Trade-offs
  • Cell splitting is the only technique that delivers uncapped capacity growth (you can always split further), but the marginal cost rises and handover overhead scales linearly with the number of new boundaries.
  • Cell sectoring is the cheapest single upgrade in the toolkit; it's the textbook first step for an operator running out of capacity on a legacy tower.
  • Microzone deployment avoids the handover storm of splitting by mapping every zone under one central BTS, but it shifts cost from towers to fibre runs and centralised baseband racks: economically attractive only in dense indoor venues.
Examiner Trap — Over-sectoring A common over-engineering mistake: dividing a cell into 6 or 12 sectors instead of the standard 3. While this further reduces co-channel interference (S/I improves an additional ~3 dB at 6-sector), it multiplies handoff load (every user crossing each sector boundary inside the same cell triggers an intra-cell handover) and severely degrades trunking efficiency (Erlang-B blocking on a pool of 10 channels is far worse than on a pool of 60). Stick to 3-sector 120° as the practical sweet spot unless dense urban traffic demands otherwise.
Modern Relevance

2026 networks heavily favour 5G small-cell densification: a refinement of cell splitting where pico/femto cells (10–100 m radius) are deployed in malls, stadiums, and on street furniture (lamp posts, traffic signals). Massive-MIMO + 3D beamforming in 5G NR effectively realises adaptive sectoring, with 64 to 256 spatially-steered beams replacing 3 fixed sectors, so the directional pattern follows the user instead of being static. Modern Distributed Antenna Systems (DAS) productise the microzone concept across enterprise indoors and venues (cricket stadiums, airports, metro systems). All three classical techniques therefore survive — just in software-defined, beam-shaped, fibre-fed form.

Conclusion

Cell splitting, sectoring, and microzone form the cellular capacity playbook. Splitting buys raw area gain at high capex; sectoring is the cheapest interference fix; microzone trades cabling for handover-free indoor capacity. Modern 5G blends all three — small cells (splitting), Massive-MIMO beams (sectoring), and centralised distributed units (microzone-style DAS) — but the underlying physics, same-channel reuse at a safe distance, has not changed since the original AMPS design.

Q6Unit 3 Highlight15M
Compare FDMA, TDMA, CDMA, and SDMA multiple access techniques in detail with a comparison table. Discuss the code separation concept in CDMA and show a worked bit-level despreading example.
Introduction

Multiple access defines how multiple subscribers share a single physical wireless channel without destructive interference. The four canonical schemes (FDMA, TDMA, CDMA, SDMA) partition the medium along four orthogonal axes: frequency, time, code, space. FDMA dominated 1G analog cellular (AMPS, TACS, NMT); TDMA became the workhorse of 2G GSM and IS-136; CDMA powered the IS-95 / CDMA2000 / WCDMA 3G family; and SDMA (long theoretical) finally arrived in mass deployment with 4G/5G Massive-MIMO. Real-world systems hybridise: GSM is FDMA + TDMA, LTE is OFDMA, 5G NR is OFDMA + SDMA + power-domain NOMA. Understanding the four base axes is the foundation for everything modern.

1 · FDMA — Frequency Division Multiple Access
  • Axis: Frequency. The total allocated bandwidth is sliced into many narrowband sub-carriers, each ~25–30 kHz wide in AMPS, separated by small guard bands.
  • Multiplexing: Each user is permanently assigned one sub-carrier for the duration of the call. The user transmits continuously on that carrier.
  • Advantage: Extremely simple analog hardware — a narrowband filter and an FM modulator are sufficient. No clock synchronisation required.
  • Disadvantage: Wastes spectrum during talker silence (the channel is held idle); guard bands between adjacent users consume 10–15 % of the band; rigid, with no on-demand data rate adaptation.
  • Real-world use: 1G AMPS (832 channels × 30 kHz across 25 MHz), TACS, NMT-450/900. Today FDMA-style partitioning survives as the carrier-grid in OFDMA.
2 · TDMA — Time Division Multiple Access
  • Axis: Time. All users share the same frequency but transmit in different time slots of a repeating frame.
  • Frame structure (GSM): One TDMA frame = 8 timeslots × 577 μs = 4.615 ms; one slot carries one burst of 156.25 bits → ~270.833 kbit/s gross on a 200 kHz carrier. Higher-level frames stack into multiframes (26 / 51) and superframes for control plus speech traffic.
  • Advantage: Flexible: a user can be assigned multiple slots per frame to bump up data rate (HSCSD, EDGE). The transmitter is bursty, so it draws less average power and battery, and the receiver can sleep between its own slots (DTX/DRX).
  • Disadvantage: Tight time synchronisation mandatory across the cell; each MS must fire its burst within a ±¼-symbol guard time. GSM solves this with Timing Advance commands from the BTS. Guard time + sync bits waste ~10 % of nominal capacity.
  • Real-world use: 2G GSM (8 slots / 200 kHz carrier), IS-136 D-AMPS (3 slots / 30 kHz), DECT, satellite VSAT.
3 · CDMA — Code Division Multiple Access
  • Axis: Code. All users transmit simultaneously on the same wideband carrier; user separation is purely mathematical, via unique pseudo-noise (PN) codes (e.g. Walsh codes).
  • Spreading gain (Gp): The data bit is XOR'd with a much faster chip sequence; typical chip rate W = 1.2288 Mcps (IS-95), data rate R = 9.6 kbps → G_p = W/R = 1.2288 × 10⁶ / 9.6 × 10³ = 128 ≈ 21 dB. This processing gain pulls the wanted signal out of the noise/interference floor.
  • Soft capacity: There is no hard cap on number of users; adding users gradually raises everyone's interference floor (graceful degradation), unlike FDMA/TDMA which hit a hard wall.
  • Disadvantage: The near-far effect: a close mobile drowns out distant ones unless aggressive closed-loop power control (~800 Hz updates in IS-95) equalises received powers at the BTS. Also requires highly accurate timing reference (often GPS-disciplined at the BTS).
  • Real-world use: 3G WCDMA, CDMA2000, IS-95. Surviving today inside 4G LTE's CDMA-based HARQ sub-layer and 5G NR's broadcast channels.
4 · SDMA — Space Division Multiple Access
  • Axis: Space. The same time-frequency resource is reused at different spatial locations through directional or beam-formed antennas; adaptive electromagnetic isolation replaces frequency/time/code separation.
  • Mechanism: A phased-array antenna with N elements can synthesise N independent beams by adjusting per-element amplitude and phase weights. Massive-MIMO arrays in 5G NR use 64 to 256 elements per panel, supporting tens of simultaneous user beams on the same PRB.
  • Advantage: Dramatic spectral-efficiency boost: the same band is reused N times per cell. Reduces co-channel interference at the source (not just at the receiver).
  • Disadvantage: High array hardware complexity, channel-state-information (CSI) feedback overhead, and reliance on rich multipath (line-of-sight scenarios can collapse the spatial dimension). Also vulnerable to user mobility: beams must be re-steered continuously.
  • Real-world use: 5G NR Massive-MIMO, IEEE 802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6) downlink MU-MIMO, satellite spot-beam systems (Starlink, OneWeb).

CDMA Code Separation & Orthogonality

In CDMA, every active mobile is assigned a unique Pseudo-Noise (PN) code vector from a set of orthogonal Walsh codes. The inner product of any two distinct codes $C_A \cdot C_B = 0$, while $C_A \cdot C_A = N$ (where $N$ is chip length). This enables multiple signals to share the same frequency band simultaneously without interfering.

Worked Bit-Level CDMA Example

User A has code C_A = [1, -1, 1, -1]. User B has code C_B = [1, 1, -1, -1]. Note: $C_A \cdot C_B = 1(1) - 1(1) - 1(1) + 1(1) = 0$ (orthogonal).

Step 1
User A wants to send bit 1 (represented as $+1$) ⇒ Transmits S_A = +1 × C_A = [1, -1, 1, -1].
Step 2
User B wants to send bit -1 ⇒ Transmits S_B = -1 × C_B = [-1, -1, 1, 1].
Step 3
Combined over-the-air signal: S = S_A + S_B = [0, -2, 2, 0].
Step 4
Receiver A recovers A's bit by taking the inner product of S and $C_A$:
Result = S · C_A = 0(1) + (-2)(-1) + 2(1) + 0(-1) = 0 + 2 + 2 + 0 = +4.
Divide by chip length N = 4: +4 / 4 = +1 (Bit 1 successfully recovered!).
Worked Numerical — CDMA Spreading Gain
Step 1
IS-95 system uses chip rate W = 1.2288 Mcps and voice data rate R = 9.6 kbps.
Step 2
Processing gain Gp = W / R = 1.2288 × 10⁶ / 9.6 × 10³ = 128.
Step 3
In dB: Gp(dB) = 10·log₁₀(128) = 21.07 dB. This is how much the receiver suppresses uncorrelated interference relative to the wanted signal.

Boxed answer: Gp = 128 ≈ 21 dB processing gain — enough to operate at receiver input SNR as low as −15 dB (below the noise floor) and still recover the bit.

Examiner Trap — TDMA time sync TDMA's biggest practical Achilles heel is tight time synchronisation: every mobile must fire its burst within a ±¼-symbol guard time of its assigned slot, or it collides with neighbours. GSM mobiles correct this via Timing Advance commands from the BTS. Forgetting to mention sync & guard time for TDMA loses 2 marks — examiners check for it specifically.
Modern Relevance

4G LTE and 5G NR use OFDMA (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access) — a refined FDMA where subcarriers are mutually orthogonal, eliminating guard bands. The uplink in LTE uses SC-FDMA for lower PAPR. Massive-MIMO in 5G is essentially SDMA at scale (64–256 antennas), and the next 6G generation (3GPP Rel-20+) explores non-orthogonal multiple access (NOMA), with multiple users on the same time-frequency resource separated by power level.

Conclusion

FDMA, TDMA, CDMA, and SDMA represent the four orthogonal partitionings of the wireless medium; CDMA's mathematical orthogonality unlocked the 3G capacity revolution, and SDMA's spatial dimension now powers 5G Massive-MIMO. Real systems combine multiple axes (OFDMA + SDMA + power-domain NOMA) to squeeze maximum capacity from finite spectrum.
