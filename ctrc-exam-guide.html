01Critical Thinking
& Rhetoric

Reasoning, argumentation, persuasion — the foundations of critical thought.

Critical Thinking Basics

Definition Critical thinking is the intellectually disciplined process of actively conceptualising, applying, analysing, synthesising, and evaluating information gathered from observation, experience, or reasoning — to reach a well-reasoned conclusion.
6 Key Elements — OAIEES
  • Observation — direct, objective perception of facts using the senses
  • Analysis — breaking complex information into components to understand relationships
  • Inference — drawing logical conclusions from available evidence
  • Evaluation — assessing credibility, relevance, and strength of evidence and arguments
  • Explanation — clearly communicating reasoning and conclusions
  • Self-Regulation — monitoring and correcting one's own thinking process
Why it matters CT prevents blind acceptance of misinformation. Foundation of academic writing, professional communication, and sound judgment.

Inductive & Deductive Reasoning 10M — BCA25+BSC25

🔼 Inductive

Specific observations → General conclusion

  • Collect specific cases
  • Identify a pattern
  • Form a general rule
  • Conclusion is probable, not certain
  • New evidence can overturn it
Example: Every visit to that coffee shop, order takes 10 min. → "That shop always takes 10 minutes."
🔽 Deductive

General premise → Specific conclusion

  • Start with an accepted premise
  • Apply to a specific case
  • Reach a logically certain conclusion
  • Conclusion is guaranteed if premises true
  • Used in mathematics, law, formal logic
Classic: All men are mortal (P1). Socrates is a man (P2). ∴ Socrates is mortal.
Inductive vs Deductive — Key Differences
ParameterInductiveDeductive
DirectionSpecific → GeneralGeneral → Specific
CertaintyProbable onlyCertain (if premises true)
Primary useScientific research, discoveryMathematics, law, logic
WeaknessRisk of over-generalisationDepends on premise validity
Prof Pattern 10M: define both + give examples (coffee shop + Socrates) + comparison table. Always write both examples by name — appeared in notes verbatim.

De Bono's Six Thinking Hats 5M — BCA25+BSC25

Concept Developed by Edward de Bono. Six coloured hats, each representing a distinct mode of thinking. Everyone "wears" the same hat simultaneously — parallel thinking — reducing conflict and ensuring all angles are explored.
Mnemonic — WR-BY-GBWhite Red · Black Yellow · Green Blue
Importance of Six Hats (7 points for 10M)
  • Enables parallel thinking — all think in same direction, reducing adversarial debate
  • Separates ego from ideas — roles, not personal attacks
  • Saves time — discussions stay focused and structured
  • Ensures thorough risk analysis through dedicated Black Hat time
  • Encourages creativity with protected Green Hat space
  • Improves group decision-making by covering all perspectives systematically
  • Universally applicable — business meetings, debates, academic discussions, product design
Prof Pattern 5M = list all 6 hats with one line each. 10M = add 7-point importance + example scenario. Part-A most commonly asks about Blue Hat (process/control role).

Rhetorical Situation — Purpose & Audience

Definition The full context in which communication occurs — combining the speaker, audience, topic, purpose, and context (time, place, culture). Every piece of communication is shaped by its rhetorical situation.
Purpose — 4 Types
  • To inform: Present facts objectively — reports, lectures, news
  • To persuade: Change beliefs or prompt action — speeches, ads, debates
  • To entertain: Engage and interest — stories, humour, creative writing
  • To inspire: Motivate emotional response — graduation addresses, motivational talks
Audience Types & Adaptations
  • Expert: Technical terms accepted; minimal explanation; deep analysis expected
  • General: Avoid jargon; use analogies; assume no specialist knowledge
  • Hostile: Acknowledge counter-arguments first; build credibility before persuading
  • Sympathetic: Assume shared values; more direct emotional appeal
Key Insight Audience analysis shapes everything: vocabulary level, tone (formal/casual), structure (summary-first vs. deep-dive), and type of evidence (data vs. stories).

Rhetorical Strategies BSC25 Q1(i)

  • Compare: Show similarities to clarify an unfamiliar concept via analogy
  • Contrast: Highlight differences to prevent confusion between similar concepts
  • Classify: Group items into categories by shared characteristics — makes complex info manageable
  • Describe: Use specific sensory/factual details to make abstract ideas concrete
  • Cause & Effect: Explain what caused something and what resulted — shows logical links
  • Definition: Clarify meaning of technical or abstract terms before building arguments

Alliteration & Amplification

  • Alliteration: Repetition of the same consonant sound at the start of consecutive or closely connected words. Used for rhythm, memorability, and emphasis.
    "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers."
  • Amplification: A rhetorical device where an idea is expanded with added detail or elaboration to increase impact. Restates an idea with progressive intensification.
    "He was tired — exhausted, bone-weary, utterly drained, barely able to lift his pen."
Part-A Tip Alliteration = same starting sound + consecutive words. Amplification = restate + add detail/intensity. One example each is sufficient for 1.5M.

Definition A checklist for evaluating the reliability and credibility of information sources. Developed at CSU Chico Library. CRAAP = Currency · Relevance · Authority · Accuracy · Purpose.
LetterCriterionKey questions to ask
CCurrencyWhen published/updated? Recent enough for your topic? (Tech/medicine = needs recent sources)
RRelevanceDirectly addresses your question? Depth appropriate — not too basic or advanced?
AAuthorityWho wrote it? Qualifications? Peer-reviewed? Reputable publisher?
AAccuracyEvidence provided? Sources cited? Can facts be independently verified?

Credible Sources & Bias

Characteristics of Credible Sources
  • Written by qualified experts with verifiable credentials
  • Published in peer-reviewed journals or by reputed academic publishers
  • Contains citations and references for all major claims
  • Presents a balanced viewpoint; acknowledges limitations
  • Recently published or updated (especially for fast-changing fields)
Bias — Definition A systematic tendency to favour one perspective, often distorting how information is presented or interpreted — consciously or unconsciously.
  • Confirmation bias: Seeking only information that confirms existing beliefs
  • Selection bias: Cherry-picking data that supports your view; omitting contradictory evidence
  • Framing bias: How wording shapes perception — same facts, different framing = different conclusions
  • Source bias: Relying on sources with vested interests (sponsored articles, industry-funded research)
Alternative Viewpoints Actively seeking opposing perspectives: (1) reduces blind spots, (2) strengthens own argument by anticipating objections, (3) leads to more credible, balanced analysis — essential in academic writing.

Observation & Inference

👁 Observation
  • Direct, objective perception using senses
  • Factual — can be independently verified
  • No interpretation or judgment
  • Describes only what is directly perceived
"The ground is wet."
💡 Inference
  • Conclusion drawn from observations
  • Goes beyond what is directly perceived
  • May be correct or incorrect
  • Involves interpretation and reasoning
"It rained recently." (could also be sprinklers)
Role in CT Confusing observation with inference is a common thinking error. Good CT means clearly separating what you see from what you conclude — then testing inferences against additional evidence.

Problem Solving 15M — High Probability

Definition A systematic cognitive process of identifying, analysing, and resolving a problem to achieve a desired outcome. Integrates critical thinking, creativity, and decision-making.
7 Key Elements
  1. Define the problem: Articulate the root problem, not symptoms. "Students failing" = symptom; "lack of study methods" = root.
  2. Analyse root causes: Use 5-Whys technique — ask "why?" five times to trace cause to its source.
  3. Generate alternatives: Brainstorm multiple solutions without judging. Quantity before quality.
  4. Evaluate options: Assess each against criteria: feasibility, cost, time, impact, risk.
  5. Select best solution: Choose option that best meets all criteria; consider long-term consequences.
  6. Implement: Execute with clear action plan — responsibilities, milestones, resource allocation.
  7. Review & evaluate: Check if problem resolved. Document lessons for future reference.

The STAR Method 15M — BCA25 Q4 + BSC25 Q4

Definition A structured technique for answering behavioural interview questions by narrating a real experience using four components: Situation · Task · Action · Result.
Prof Pattern — 15M Define STAR + explain each component + write a complete model answer for a named question. Examiners look for: correct framework, realistic story, "Action" uses "I" not "we", measurable or meaningful result.
Model Answer — "Tell me about a challenge you faced at college"

Situation: In my third semester, our group of four students was working on a database management project due in two weeks. Midway through, two team members fell ill and became unavailable, leaving only myself and one other student to complete the entire work.

Task: I needed to ensure the project was submitted on time without sacrificing quality, while managing the doubled workload and keeping my remaining teammate motivated.

Action: I immediately reassessed all outstanding tasks and redistributed them based on our respective strengths. I created a day-by-day micro-schedule and held 15-minute daily check-ins. I personally handled the ER diagram and schema design, while guiding my teammate through the SQL query writing. I communicated proactively with the professor about the situation.

Result: We submitted on the original deadline and received a B+ grade. The professor commended our structured approach in class. I learned that systematic planning and communication under pressure are more valuable than technical knowledge alone.

The 3 Ps of Conflict Resolution 4× across BCA+BSC

Framework A structured approach to resolving conflict by addressing three core dimensions — Problem, Perspective, and Process — in sequence.

Five Conflict Management Strategies BCA25 Q7 + BSC papers

Thomas-Kilmann Model Five strategies based on two dimensions: assertiveness (pursuing own goals) and cooperativeness (satisfying others' goals).
Mnemonic — AC-ACCAvoid · Compete · Accommodate · Compromise · Collaborate
Avoid
Low assertiveness + Low cooperation. Withdraw; neither side's goals pursued. Use when: issue is trivial or emotions too high. Risk: problem stays unresolved.
Compete
High assertiveness + Low cooperation. Assert own position forcefully. Use when: emergency requiring quick decisive action. Risk: damages relationships.
Accommodate
Low assertiveness + High cooperation. Give in to others' needs. Use when: relationship matters more than the issue. Risk: may breed resentment.
Compromise
Moderate + Moderate. Both give something up; middle ground. Use when: quick resolution needed, equal power. Risk: neither fully satisfied.
Collaborate ★
High assertiveness + High cooperation. Best strategy. Find solution that fully satisfies all parties. Use for: important long-term relationships. Most durable outcome.
Prof Pattern — 15M State Thomas-Kilmann model → all 5 strategies (definition + when to use + risk) → add 3Ps framework → conclude that Collaboration is the ideal strategy for sustainable resolution.

Group Discussion (GD)

Definition A structured discussion among a group on a given topic, used to assess communication, analytical thinking, leadership, and teamwork skills.
Effective GD Skills
  • Initiate confidently: Start with a relevant definition or statistic — strong first impression
  • Active listening: Acknowledge others' points before building on them — "Building on what X said…"
  • Data-backed points: Use statistics, named examples, case studies to support arguments
  • Constructive disagreement: "I see your point; however, the evidence suggests…" — never personal
  • Body language: Eye contact with the group, not just the evaluator; open posture
  • Summarise: If discussion drifts — "To summarise the key points so far…"
GD Mistakes to Avoid Interrupting others · monopolising discussion · going completely silent · personal remarks · vague points with no examples or data.

(a) Define critical thinking. List any two key elements.

Critical thinking is the disciplined, reflective process of analysing information, questioning assumptions, weighing evidence and forming a reasoned judgment instead of accepting claims at face value. John Dewey (1910) called it "reflective thinking." Two key elements: analysis (breaking arguments into parts) and evaluation (judging evidence quality).

(b) What is inductive reasoning? Give one example.

Inductive reasoning moves from specific observations to a general conclusion. The conclusion is probable, not certain — new evidence can overturn it. Example: "Every swan I have seen is white → all swans are white" — overturned when black swans were found in Australia (1697).

(c) Differentiate between observation and inference.

An observation is a direct sensory fact ("the sky is grey, the road is wet"). An inference is a conclusion drawn from observation ("it has rained recently"). Observation = data; inference = interpretation. Confusing the two is the root of biased reporting.

(d) What does the Blue Hat represent in De Bono's Six Thinking Hats?

The Blue Hat is the process-control hat — the conductor of the thinking session. It sets the agenda, decides which hat the group wears next, manages time and draws the final summary. Often called "thinking about thinking."

(e) Define rhetorical situation and name its components.

The rhetorical situation, theorised by Lloyd Bitzer (1968), is the full context within which any act of communication occurs. Five components: Speaker, Audience, Topic, Purpose, Context (time, place, culture). Mis-aligning any one causes the message to misfire.

(f) Expand CRAAP. What does Authority mean in the CRAAP test?

CRAAP = Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose (Sarah Blakeslee, CSU Chico Meriam Library, 2004). Authority asks: who created the source, what are the author's credentials, is the publisher reputable, is it peer-reviewed? A peer-reviewed journal outranks an anonymous blog.

(g) Define alliteration with one example.

Alliteration is the repetition of the same initial consonant sound in two or more nearby words. Example: "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers." It heightens rhythm, memorability and audience attention in speeches and slogans ("Coca-Cola", "PayPal").

(h) What does STAR stand for in interview skills?

STAR = Situation, Task, Action, Result. A structured framework for answering behavioural interview questions ("tell me about a time when…") with one concrete past example. Used by Google, Amazon and Microsoft hiring panels.

(i) State the 3 Ps of conflict resolution.

Problem — define the real issue, separating it from personalities. People — manage relationships and emotions through active listening. Process — agree on a method (negotiation, mediation, arbitration) with steps and timeline. All three must be addressed for a durable solution.

(j) Name any three conflict management strategies.

From the Thomas-Kilmann model (1974): Compromise (split the difference under time pressure), Collaborate (win-win, best for long-term relationships) and Avoid (withdraw when the issue is trivial or timing is wrong).

Explain inductive and deductive reasoning with suitable examples. How do they differ? How do these methods contribute to critical thinking?
Introduction

Reasoning is the engine of critical thinking. Two foundational forms are inductive (popularised by Francis Bacon in Novum Organum, 1620, as the cornerstone of empirical science) and deductive (formalised by Aristotle in Prior Analytics, c. 350 BCE, as the syllogism). Both move between specific facts and general rules but in opposite directions.

1. Inductive Reasoning — Specific → General
  • Definition: Drawing a general conclusion from a finite set of specific observations. The conclusion is probable, never certain — fresh evidence can overturn it.
  • Example: Every swan observed in Europe is white → therefore all swans are white. (This held until 1697 when Willem de Vlamingh found black swans in Australia — classic refutation of induction.)
  • Where used: Scientific research, market research, machine learning models, medical diagnosis from symptoms.
2. Deductive Reasoning — General → Specific
  • Definition: Starting from a general principle (premise) and applying it to a specific case to reach a logically certain conclusion — if the premises are true, the conclusion must follow.
  • Classic syllogism: All men are mortal (P1). Socrates is a man (P2). ∴ Socrates is mortal (C). The form guarantees the conclusion when premises are true.
  • Where used: Mathematics, formal logic, legal arguments, software verification.
Comparison
ParameterInductiveDeductive
DirectionSpecific → GeneralGeneral → Specific
CertaintyProbable; revisableCertain if premises true
Primary useEmpirical sciences, ML, pollingMathematics, law, formal logic
FounderFrancis Bacon (1620)Aristotle (c. 350 BCE)
Failure modeHasty generalisation (too few cases)Invalid / false premise
Contribution to Critical Thinking
  • Induction trains the mind to gather evidence systematically and stay open to revision — essential when reading research, polls or news.
  • Deduction trains the mind to test internal consistency: if I accept these premises, what must follow? It exposes hidden assumptions in arguments.
  • Strong critical thinkers cycle between the two: induce a hypothesis from data, then deduce its consequences and test them (Karl Popper's hypothetico-deductive model).
Common Pitfalls Inductive: over-generalising from a small sample ("I met 3 rude Delhi drivers → all Delhi drivers are rude"). Deductive: conclusion looks valid but a premise is false ("All politicians lie → X is a politician → X lies" — invalid because premise 1 is a generalisation, not a rule).

Q2(b)★★ BCA25 Q2(b) · BCA25 Q1(b) · BSC25 Q1(g)5M
Discuss Edward de Bono's Six Thinking Hats in detail. How does this method improve problem-solving and decision-making in a team setting?

The Six Thinking Hats, developed by Maltese psychologist Edward de Bono in his 1985 book of the same name, is a parallel thinking tool. Instead of adversarial debate, the whole team "wears" the same coloured hat at the same time, looking at the problem from one angle, then switches together — separating ego from idea.

  • ⬜ White Hat — Facts: What information do we have? What is missing? Pure data, no opinion.
  • 🟥 Red Hat — Emotions: Gut feelings, intuition, hunches. No justification needed.
  • ⬛ Black Hat — Caution: Risks, weaknesses, why it may fail. The devil's advocate.
  • 🟨 Yellow Hat — Benefits: Optimism, value, why it will work.
  • 🟩 Green Hat — Creativity: New ideas, alternatives, lateral thinking.
  • 🟦 Blue Hat — Process: Conductor — sets agenda, sequences hats, summarises.

Team benefit: Reduces conflict (everyone criticises together under Black Hat, no one is "the negative person"), prevents premature judgment, ensures balanced coverage of risks and opportunities, and speeds up meetings — IBM, Pepsi and the Norwegian government have all formally adopted it.

Mnemonic — "WRBYGB" · "White Reads, Black Beware, Yellow Believes, Green Grows, Red Reacts, Blue Bosses"

Q3★★★ HIGHEST · BCA25 Q4 + BSC25 Q4 + BCA25 Q1(g,h)15M
Explain the STAR method in detail. How does each step (Situation, Task, Action, Result) contribute to delivering an effective interview response? How can STAR be tailored to fit a job description? Provide a complete example to illustrate its application.
Introduction

The STAR method is a structured framework for answering behavioural interview questions — the "tell me about a time when…" style of questions popularised by Tom Janz's Behavioural Interviewing (1982) and operationalised into the STAR acronym by Development Dimensions International (DDI) in the 1970s. Today it is the dominant interview format at Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Goldman Sachs and most of the Fortune 500.

The Four Steps and Their Contribution
  • S — Situation: Set the scene in 1–2 sentences. Where? When? Who was involved? Gives the interviewer essential context without burning time. Contribution: grounds the rest of the answer in a verifiable scenario.
  • T — Task: Your specific responsibility or objective. Not the team's task — yours. Contribution: clarifies what success looked like for you, so Action and Result are measurable against it.
  • A — Action: The single biggest chunk (~50% of the answer). Use "I" not "we". Describe step-by-step what you did, what skills you applied, what trade-offs you weighed. Contribution: this is where competencies (leadership, ownership, problem-solving) actually get demonstrated. Skipping detail here is the #1 reason candidates fail STAR.
  • R — Result: Quantify the outcome (numbers, %, time saved, marks scored). State one lesson learned. End positively. Contribution: proves impact and shows reflection — the trait recruiters value most.
STAR — Component Breakdown Table
StepWhat to includeTime shareCommon mistake
SituationContext: where, when, who, scale~15%Drowning in context, burning 60% here
TaskYour specific objective; what success looked like~15%Stating the team's task instead of yours
ActionStep-by-step "I did X, I decided Y", skills applied, trade-offs~50%Using "we" — interviewer cannot score the team

Tailoring STAR to the Job Description
  • Mine the JD for keywords — e.g. "stakeholder management", "data-driven", "ownership". These are the competencies the panel is scoring against.
  • Pick a Situation that lets you exercise the JD's top 2 keywords. If the JD says "cross-functional collaboration", pick a story with multi-team coordination, not a solo project.
  • Mirror the JD's vocabulary in your Action. If the JD says "data-driven", explicitly say "I pulled the conversion-rate data and decided…" not "I felt that…".
  • Quantify Result in the JD's currency — engineering roles want latency / uptime / lines reduced; sales roles want revenue / pipeline / retention.
Complete Model Answer — "Tell me about a time you led a team through a challenge."

Situation: In my third semester (Oct 2024), our group of four was working on a DBMS final project due in 14 days. Midway through, two members fell ill and were unavailable for a full week.

Task: As the de-facto lead I had to ship a working database schema, query layer and demo on the original deadline at submission-ready quality — without slipping or asking for an extension that would cost us 10% marks.

Action: I (1) re-scoped the project, cutting two optional features the rubric did not score; (2) drafted a day-by-day plan in Notion with a 15-minute daily standup at 9 PM; (3) personally took ownership of the ER diagram and schema design while pair-programming SQL queries with my remaining teammate to upskill her; (4) emailed the professor proactively about the situation and got pre-approval for our reduced scope; (5) booked a 30-minute slot with a TA mid-week to validate our schema and caught a 3NF violation before it cost us marks.

Result: We submitted on the original deadline and scored 86/100 (top quartile). The professor commented our approach was "the most professional in the cohort." Personally, I learned that systematic scope-reduction and proactive stakeholder communication are more valuable than working longer hours.

Modern Relevance

Amazon's "14 Leadership Principles" interview format is essentially STAR — every behavioural question maps to a principle and the rubric scores S/T/A/R independently. Google's "structured interviewing" similarly forces STAR responses. Knowing STAR is now table-stakes for any campus placement at a product or consulting firm.

Conclusion

STAR converts vague self-praise into evidence. Its discipline forces candidates to ground every claim in a concrete past situation, demonstrate the action they personally took, and prove impact with numbers — a far more reliable signal than rehearsed adjectives. Mastering STAR is mastering the language of modern hiring.

Common Pitfalls (1) Drowning in Situation — burning 60% of the answer on context. (2) Using "we" in Action — interviewers cannot score the team. (3) No quantified Result — "it went well" is worthless; "scored 86/100" is auditable. (4) Picking a story that does not match the asked competency.

Q4(a)★★★ HIGHEST · BCA25 Q3(b) + BSC25 Q3(b) + BCA25 Q1(d)10M
How can you determine whether a source is credible? Discuss the key criteria — currency, relevance, authority, accuracy and purpose — with examples. How would you apply the CRAAP test to a social media post?
Introduction

In a world where 500 hours of video are uploaded to YouTube every minute and X/Twitter publishes ~500 million posts a day, evaluating source credibility is no longer a librarian's job — it is a survival skill. The CRAAP test, developed by Sarah Blakeslee and her colleagues at the Meriam Library, California State University Chico, in 2004, is the gold-standard checklist. CRAAP = Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose.

The Five Criteria — with diagnostic questions and examples
  • Currency — How recent? In fast-moving fields (technology, medicine, COVID-19), anything older than 2–3 years may already be obsolete. Ask: When was it published? When was it last updated? Are links live?
    Example: A 2018 article on "best machine-learning frameworks" is largely outdated — PyTorch dominance and the transformer revolution post-date it.
  • Relevance — Does it answer my specific question at the right depth? Ask: Is the audience me (general / scholar / child)? Does it dive deep enough or is it a 200-word summary?
    Example: A 5-minute YouTube explainer on quantum computing is irrelevant for a postgraduate paper but perfect for a school assembly talk.
  • Authority — Who wrote / published this? Ask: Author's credentials, institutional affiliation, publisher's reputation, peer review?
    Example: A vaccine claim on a Lancet peer-reviewed paper outranks the same claim on a personal blog — even if the blogger is loud.
  • Accuracy — Are claims supported and verifiable? Ask: Are sources cited? Can the numbers be reproduced from raw data? Are there typos, broken citations or unsourced quotes?
    Example: Wikipedia is hit-or-miss on Accuracy — popular pages are well-cited; obscure pages can carry unsourced claims for years.
  • Purpose — Why does this exist? Inform, persuade, sell, entertain? Ask: Who funded it? Is there a sponsorship banner? Are emotional words doing the work that evidence should?
    Example: "10 reasons milk is dangerous" on a vegan-product website fails Purpose — the article exists to sell almond milk.
Worked Example — Applying CRAAP to a Social Media Post

Suppose a viral X/Twitter post claims: "Drinking lemon water at 5 AM cures diabetes — a Harvard study proved it." Apply CRAAP:

CriterionQuestion to askVerdict on the post
CurrencyWhen was the alleged study published?No date cited → fail
RelevanceDoes a tweet replace a clinical trial summary?Too shallow → fail
AuthorityWho is the account? Are they a doctor? Is "Harvard study" linked?Anonymous influencer, no link → fail
AccuracySearch PubMed / Harvard's site for the study — does it exist?No study found → fail
PurposeDoes the bio link to a lemon-water product?Yes, affiliate funnel → fail

Five fails → reject. Compare with a WHO press release on the same topic: dated, written by named experts, peer-reviewed studies linked, and the WHO has no product to sell — passes all five.

Common Pitfall Trusting Authority alone. The New York Times is authoritative but still has editorial Purpose (political slant). Always cross-check at least 3 of the 5 criteria — never let one impressive credential override the rest.

What is bias in content analysis? How does it impact the evaluation of arguments? Explain strategies to identify and eliminate bias in information.

Bias is a systematic tendency to favour one perspective over others, distorting how information is selected, framed or interpreted — often unconsciously. Daniel Kahneman's Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011) catalogues over 30 such cognitive biases.

  • Confirmation bias: seeking only evidence that supports an existing belief (e.g. WhatsApp echo chambers).
  • Selection / sampling bias: cherry-picking convenient data (e.g. quoting only successful entrepreneurs to claim "drop-outs always succeed").
  • Framing bias: wording shapes perception ("95% survival" vs "5% mortality" — same fact, opposite feeling).

Impact on argument evaluation: biased sources produce false premises → even deductively valid arguments lead to wrong conclusions; biased framing manipulates emotional response; biased sampling lets propaganda masquerade as data.

Strategies to identify and eliminate bias:

  • Read laterally — open 3+ sources on opposite sides (Stanford fact-checker technique).
  • Check funding & affiliation — who paid for the study?
  • Look for emotional language — bias hides in adjectives ("shocking", "devastating").
  • Apply the CRAAP test — Purpose criterion targets bias directly.
  • Seek alternative viewpoints — they reveal blind spots, anticipate objections, strengthen the final judgment.

Explain the 3 Ps of conflict resolution — Problem, People and Process. How do they help in effectively managing and resolving conflicts? Discuss the five conflict resolution strategies — avoid, compromise, accommodate, compete and collaborate — with examples of when each is appropriate.
Introduction

Conflict in workplaces, classrooms and online communities is unavoidable — surveys (CPP Global, 2008) found employees spend ~2.8 hours per week dealing with conflict. Two complementary frameworks dominate: the 3 Ps (diagnostic — what to address) and the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (Kenneth W. Thomas + Ralph H. Kilmann, 1974) which gives five strategies plotted on a 2-axis grid.

Part 1 — The 3 Ps of Conflict Resolution
  • P1 — Problem: Define the actual issue, separating it from the people involved. Surface the underlying interest beneath the stated position. How it helps: stops the conflict from drifting into personality attacks.
    Example: Two teammates argue over deadline allocation. The Problem is unbalanced workload distribution, not that "Riya is lazy."
  • P2 — People: Manage the human side — emotions, relationships, communication style. Use active listening, validate each party's feelings, ensure everyone is heard. How it helps: reduces defensiveness, opens space for honest dialogue.
    Example: Before debating the schedule, the team lead lets each person describe how the workload felt to them.
  • P3 — Process: Agree on a method — negotiation, mediation, arbitration — with clear steps, timeline, decision authority and follow-up. How it helps: turns vague "we'll talk about it" into a contract.
    Example: The team agrees: "By Friday we will re-distribute tasks; the project manager has final say; review again in 2 weeks."
Part 2 — Five Conflict Resolution Strategies (Thomas-Kilmann)

The two axes are Assertiveness (how much I push for my own goal) and Cooperativeness (how much I accommodate the other party's goal).

StrategyWhat it meansUse when…Risk
AvoidPostpone or withdraw; neither side's goals pursuedIssue is trivial, timing is wrong, emotions are too hotProblem festers, returns later bigger
CompeteAssert your position; one side winsEmergency, safety, ethical line, unpopular but right callDamages relationships, breeds resentment
AccommodateGive in to preserve harmonyIssue matters far more to the other party; goodwill capital neededResentment if used too often; opponent learns to push
CompromiseBoth concede something; quick mid-pointEqual power, tight deadline, temporary fix neededNeither fully satisfied; sub-optimal solution
Collaborate ★Work jointly for a win-win that satisfies bothImportant long-term relationship, complex issue, time availableTime-intensive; needs trust and skill
Modern Relevance

In hybrid and remote work (post-2020), text-based conflict (Slack, email) now outnumbers face-to-face disagreements. Without tone, even small frictions escalate — making the 3Ps even more vital. Companies like Pixar, Bridgewater Associates and the Indian Army have formal conflict-resolution protocols built explicitly on 3P + Thomas-Kilmann.

Conclusion

The 3 Ps tell you what to address (issue, emotions, method) — the five strategies tell you how to address it. A skilled manager diagnoses with the 3 Ps then picks the appropriate strategy from the Thomas-Kilmann grid based on the stakes and the relationship. Used together they convert destructive conflict into productive disagreement.

What is problem solving? Discuss the key strategies / elements of effective problem solving with examples. How does problem solving connect to critical thinking?
Introduction

Problem solving is the systematic cognitive process of identifying, analysing and resolving a difficulty in order to achieve a desired outcome. It combines critical thinking (analysis + evaluation), creativity (alternative generation) and decision-making (selecting + acting). Two classical models dominate: George Polya's How to Solve It (1945) — Understand → Plan → Execute → Look Back — and the IDEAL model of Bransford and Stein (1984) — Identify, Define, Explore, Act, Look back. The seven-strategy version below synthesises both with the modern Design Thinking cycle (IDEO, popularised 2009).

Seven Key Strategies / Elements
  1. Define the problem clearly — distinguish symptom from root cause. Example: "Students failing the unit" is a symptom; "no structured revision plan in the last week" is the root.
  2. Analyse root causes — use the 5-Whys technique (Sakichi Toyoda, Toyota): ask "why?" five times in succession to drill from surface to source. Example: Why did the deploy fail? → Bad config. Why? → No staging test. Why? → Pipeline lacks staging step.
  3. Generate alternatives — brainstorm quantity before quality (Osborn's rules, 1953); use Green Hat thinking from De Bono. Premature filtering kills creative options.
  4. Evaluate options — score each alternative against criteria: feasibility, cost, time, risk, impact. A weighted decision matrix turns gut feel into auditable choice.
  5. Select the best solution — pick the option with the highest weighted score and compatible long-term consequences. Document the reasoning.
  6. Implement with a plan — assign owners, set milestones, allocate resources, plan for known risks. Without an action plan a great solution stays on paper.
  7. Review and learn (Look Back) — measure outcome vs target, conduct a blameless post-mortem, document lessons in a wiki / runbook so the next team does not repeat the mistake.
Comparison of Classical Problem-Solving Models

Worked Example — Falling Internal Marks in BCA-IV

Define: Average internal mark dropped from 78 to 64 in one semester. Analyse (5-Whys): Why? Sessional answers were thin → Why? Students copy from notes → Why? Notes are unstructured → root: no model-answer library exists. Generate: 4 options — peer notes pool / faculty model answers / past-paper drills / hire tutor. Evaluate: peer pool fails Accuracy, tutor fails Cost → faculty model + drills win. Select + Implement: faculty publishes 2 model answers per unit; students do 1 drill paper weekly. Review: next semester average rebounds to 75.

Connection to Critical Thinking

Problem solving is critical thinking applied. Critical thinking supplies the analytical engine (questioning assumptions, weighing evidence, detecting bias) which the Define, Analyse and Evaluate stages depend on. Conversely, problem-solving disciplines critical thinking by forcing it toward an actionable decision rather than endless deliberation.

Common Pitfalls (1) Jumping straight to Solution without Defining — solving the wrong problem efficiently. (2) Falling in love with the first idea (anchoring bias). (3) Skipping Review — repeating the same mistake next quarter.
Modern Relevance

Design Thinking (IDEO, Tim Brown 2009) — Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test — is the dominant problem-solving framework in product companies (Apple, Airbnb, ISRO's user-research division). It is the same 7-stage cycle re-wrapped for user-centred contexts.

Conclusion

Effective problem solving is not a single skill but a disciplined cycle. Following the seven stages — backed by Polya, IDEAL and Design Thinking — turns ambiguous difficulties into structured, evidence-based action and converts experience into organisational learning.

What is the rhetorical situation? Explain its key elements — purpose, audience, topic and context — using an example to illustrate their importance in effective communication.
Introduction

The concept of rhetorical situation was formalised by American rhetorician Lloyd F. Bitzer in his landmark 1968 essay "The Rhetorical Situation" (Philosophy & Rhetoric). Bitzer argued that no speech, essay or advertisement exists in a vacuum — it is always a response to a specific context. Earlier roots trace to Aristotle's Rhetoric (c. 350 BCE), which identified three persuasive appeals — ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), logos (logic) — that operate within the rhetorical situation.

The Five Key Elements
  • Speaker (rhetor): The author or presenter. Their credibility (ethos), expertise and tone shape how the message lands. A doctor talking about vaccines carries different weight than a film star.
  • Audience: The intended receivers. Their age, expertise, beliefs and emotional state dictate vocabulary, evidence type and structure. Bitzer's key insight: rhetoric exists only when the audience can be moved to act.
  • Topic (subject): The matter being addressed. Some topics are inherently sensitive (religion, politics) and demand more pathos management; technical topics demand more logos.
  • Purpose: The communicative intent. The four canonical purposes:
    • Inform — present facts (lectures, news reports)
    • Persuade — change belief or prompt action (editorials, ads)
    • Entertain — engage and amuse (stories, talk shows)
    • Inspire — stir emotion and motivation (graduation speeches, sermons)
  • Context (Bitzer called it "exigence"): The time, place, cultural moment, occasion. The same speech about freedom delivered on 15 August in India lands differently than on 4 July in the US.
Worked Example — Same Topic, Different Audiences

Topic: Climate change. Speaker: Same environmental scientist. Purpose: Inspire action. Now vary the audience and watch the rhetorical choices shift:

AudienceVocabularyEvidence typeTone
UN climate panelTechnical (RCP 8.5, gigatonnes CO₂-eq)Peer-reviewed graphsFormal, data-led
School childrenPlain English (polar bear, melting ice)Photos, short storiesConversational, vivid
Hostile scepticsCautious, neutral framingConcede uncertainty first, then strongest 2 factsCalm, conciliatory

Same speaker, same purpose, but audience analysis reshapes vocabulary, evidence and tone. Skipping this analysis is why technical experts often fail to persuade — they speak at rather than to their audience.

Importance in Effective Communication
  • Forces the communicator to think before speaking — preventing wasted words.
  • Aligns vocabulary, tone, structure, evidence and length with what the audience needs.
  • Identifies hostility, ignorance or scepticism in advance — so the rhetor can defuse objections.
  • Picks the right channel (email vs town-hall vs Instagram reel) based on context.
  • Without it: a great message reaches the wrong audience at the wrong time and dies.

Q7(b)★ BSC25 Q1(i)5M
Write a note on rhetorical strategies of writing — explain compare, contrast, classify and describe with examples.

Rhetorical strategies are the structural moves a writer uses to develop an idea so the audience grasps it quickly. Four core strategies — taught from Aristotle through modern composition texts (Lunsford & Connors, The Everyday Writer):

  • Compare: Identify similarities between two things — usually to explain an unfamiliar concept via a familiar one (the "is like" move).
    Example: "RNA is similar to DNA in that both carry genetic information using a four-letter code." Bridges the gap from known to unknown.
  • Contrast: Highlight differences to sharpen distinctions and prevent confusion.
    Example: "Unlike inductive reasoning, which generalises from observations, deductive reasoning begins from a general principle." Useful for definitions and policy debates.
  • Classify: Group items into categories by shared characteristics — converts a long messy list into a navigable hierarchy.
    Example: "Sources of information can be classified as primary (raw data), secondary (analysis of primary) and tertiary (summaries of secondary)." Essential in research writing.
  • Describe: Build a vivid, specific picture using sensory or factual detail — turns abstract claims into concrete images the reader can see.
    Example: Instead of "climate change is bad," write "Himalayan glaciers are retreating ~30 m per year, threatening the drinking water of 1.9 billion people downstream." Numbers + named river basins = unforgettable.
Mnemonic — "4-C-D" · Compare, Contrast, Classify, Describe — pick at least two when explaining any new concept in a 10M answer.
